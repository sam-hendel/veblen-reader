# The Evolution of the Scientific Point of View

## The Postulate Nobody Mentions

Here is a thing about modern science that nobody states out loud: it assumes everything is changing, all the time, and that the changes follow one from the next. That is the whole foundation. Not a proven fact. A metaphysical preconception. An article of faith, if you like, though no scientist would call it that.

I know something about unproven assumptions. My family has been brewing beer in this same building for three generations. My grandfather knew when a fermentation was right by the smell of the room. My father could tell by the sound the foam made. I grew up believing those were facts about brewing. They were preconceptions -- useful ones, but preconceptions all the same. The new system we installed last year does not smell anything or listen to anything. It tracks consecutive change: temperature curves, pH gradients, microbial population counts, each one following from the last. It never arrives at a final answer. Every data point is a departure for the next measurement. Every optimization generates a new variable to optimize.

That is the postulate of modern science in a nutshell. Because it treats everything as consecutive change, it can never reach a final term. Every answered question breeds two new ones. Every destination turns out to be a waypoint.

## The Darwinian Shift

A hundred years ago, scientists did not see it this way. They expected to arrive somewhere. The broad shift in outlook that changed this is conventionally tagged with Darwin's name. Whether Darwin personally deserves the credit or was simply one noise among many that the wheels of civilization made going round -- that is a separate question. His name is a convenient label for a cultural shift, not necessarily a certificate of authorship.

Before that shift, science was taxonomy. The goal was to classify things into their proper resting places. Scientists looked for a primordial beginning and a definitive end -- two stable states with a transient disturbance in between. The natural laws they sought were rules of the game of causation, and the emphasis fell on the consummation. Where does this process end up? That was the question that mattered.

After Darwin -- or after whatever cultural movement his name marks -- the emphasis reversed. The process itself became the object of interest. The interval of instability and transition, which had been merely the awkward middle passage between two stable states, moved to center stage. Cumulative causation was understood as self-continuing, with no final term. Questions about where things started or where they would end simply dropped away.

I have watched this exact shift happen on my own brewery floor. My grandfather's recipes were taxonomies. This beer is a dubbel; that is a tripel. Each had a definitive form, a consummation. You were either there or you were not. The new system does not think in terms of styles or endpoints. It thinks in terms of process: this curve, then this adjustment, then this new curve. There is no final beer. There is only the next fermentation.

## Where the Point of View Comes From

Here is what most people miss: the scientific point of view is itself a product of the broader cultural situation. Or better, a by-product. The way people think about knowledge at any given time reflects the way they live.

Habits of thought come from habits of life. The community subjects its members to a discipline of habituation -- the steady pressure of daily circumstances -- and out of that discipline a consensus forms about what counts as a real explanation. The individual is a single agent. Whatever shapes them in one line of activity shapes them in all the others. You cannot spend your days immersed in one kind of work and then think in completely different terms when you sit down to theorize.

I see this in myself. Since the sensors went in, I have started thinking about everything differently. Not just beer. I catch myself tracking consecutive changes in my daughter's homework habits, in the garden soil, in the way my back aches on cold mornings. The machine discipline has colonized my thinking. My grandfather, I suspect, saw the world in terms of craft skill and personal judgment all the way down.

## Two Kinds of Knowledge

Every culture, as far back as you care to look, maintains two parallel ranges of knowledge.

The first is the higher, speculative system -- theology, cosmology, philosophy. The big picture. This range reflects the institutional structure of society: who has power, how authority works, what counts as dignified.

The second is the lower, matter-of-fact, workday knowledge -- generalizations drawn from practical experience. Water runs downhill. An edge tool cuts softer substances. Two stones weigh more than one. This range reflects the technological discipline, the habits enforced by the actual work of staying alive.

Primitive peoples hold both simultaneously. Their cosmological schemes run on animistic, dramatic lines -- creation myths, spirit-driven narratives. But underneath, they possess perfectly secure matter-of-fact knowledge. Nobody in any culture, however elaborate their theology, is confused about whether a rock will fall when you drop it. The broad scheme of things gets the dramatic treatment. The trivial daily facts are simply known, and known without question.

My brewery held both kinds of knowledge too. The higher range was family tradition and prestige -- the Cuvée named after my grandmother, the stories about the 1947 vintage, the idea that brewing is an art passed through blood. The lower range was what actually made the beer work: temperatures, timing, sanitation. For decades, the higher knowledge was what visitors heard about. The lower knowledge was what the brewers used.

## When the Gap Widens

The relationship between these two ranges of knowledge depends on the shape of institutions.

Where a coercive class hierarchy exists -- master and serf, ruler and ruled -- the higher speculative knowledge and the lower matter-of-fact knowledge pull apart. The ruling class, whose attention is absorbed by questions of authority and status, cultivates the speculative range. The working class, whose attention is absorbed by the actual behavior of actual things, accumulates the practical range. The institutional scheme creates the speculative scheme in its own image. Reality -- the officially recognized version -- becomes dissociated from actuality. The gap between what is believed on the upper floors and what is known on the ground floor can grow very wide.

Where workday experience moves to the foreground of cultural attention, the two ranges converge. And when they converge, science gets a chance to arise.

## Gods in the Shape of Institutions

You can read the institutional structure of a society right off its theology.

Peaceable agricultural peoples -- settled, relatively egalitarian, with few rigid class distinctions -- tend toward polytheism, female deities, and creation stories built around generation, germination, and growth. Their gods are relatives, not masters. Their cosmology concerns itself with crops and livelihood.

Predatory pastoral peoples -- organized around coercive hierarchy, raiding, dominance -- tend toward monotheism, male deities, and creation by fiat. God is a patriarch and a prince. The natural world runs on imposed rules. The important theological questions concern man's servile relation to divine authority, not the details of making a living.

Medieval Christendom followed the predatory pattern. The institutional scheme was coercive and hierarchical -- graded mastery and graded servitude. The theology was monarchical, despotic, built on divine fiat. Philosophy occupied itself with the corollaries of God's will. Matter-of-fact knowledge and workday information were beneath the dignity of serious inquiry. Reality was spiritual, intangible, and organized by rank. Meanwhile, on the ground, technological knowledge kept growing, but it was accepted only as practical maxim, never as science. The gap between the officially real and the actually true was wide.

## Handicraft and the New Organon

The transition to modern times brought industry to the foreground of Western life. The characteristic industry of that era was handicraft and petty trade, where the salient features were personal skill, individual initiative, and workmanlike efficiency.

This mattered enormously for science. Handicraft technology, because it ran on personal initiative and practical skill, was closer to the institutional fabric than earlier technologies had been. The two lines of habituation -- institutional and technological -- began to merge. The craft guilds embodied both. Matter-of-fact knowledge got drawn into the domain of legitimate inquiry.

The result was early modern science, and its central concept -- its new organon -- was workmanlike efficiency: a cause producing an effect. Explain how the cause makes the effect happen, through what skill or mechanism, and you have a scientific explanation. Inquiry comes to rest when that explanation is given.

My grandfather worked in exactly these terms. Why did the beer turn out well? Because he managed the fermentation with skill and judgment. The cause was his workmanship. The effect was good beer. That was a complete explanation, and it satisfied everyone.

But these early natural laws carried extra freight. They inherited a moral charge from the institutional doctrine of natural rights that grew up alongside them. The laws of nature were not just true. They were felt to be right and good -- meritorious, beneficent, carrying their own sanction. This habit of imputing moral quality to natural regularities lingered well into the nineteenth century.

## The Machine Takes Over

The handicraft preconceptions held on long after handicraft itself ceased to be the dominant technology. But a new discipline was slowly displacing them: the machine process.

I can tell you exactly what this displacement feels like, because I am living through it. The old way of thinking about our brewery was: I am a skilled craftsman, and by my initiative and judgment I produce good beer. The new way is: there is a process of consecutive change -- temperature, pH, dissolved oxygen, cell count -- and the system tracks and adjusts it continuously. There is no craftsman at the center. There is no moment where the cause has finished producing its effect. There is only unremitting process.

The machine discipline shifts science from "workmanlike efficiency of a cause producing an effect" to "impersonal process of consecutive change." In physics and chemistry, this shift has gone furthest. Stable substances dissolve into processes of inconceivably rapid consecutive change. Nothing rests. Nothing is a thing. Everything is something happening.

## The Uneven Reach

The machine-made preconceptions have naturally affected the sciences closest to the technological field first and most thoroughly. Physics, chemistry -- these have been thoroughly remodeled. But in sciences farther from the machine's domain, the older habits of thought persist.

In ethics, political theory, economics -- the so-called moral sciences -- much of the handicraft-era thinking survives intact. Natural rights doctrines, inherited from the age of handicraft, show no sign of obsolescence. Even evolution, when it appears in these fields, is construed as improvement -- a beneficent trend, a movement toward something better. That is not a scientific conclusion. It is a survival of older habits of thought, the residue of a time when natural laws were felt to be not merely true but good.

I notice this split in my own house. In the brewhouse, where the sensors run, nobody talks about the beer getting "better." They talk about the process shifting. But in the tasting room, in the marketing meetings, in the family dinners where we discuss the brewery's future, everything is framed as improvement or decline, progress or tradition, better or worse. The machine discipline governs one floor. Older preconceptions govern the other.

Whether the machine-era point of view will ever fully penetrate the moral and human sciences remains an open question. There are still habits of thought that predispose their holders to seek final answers, to find merit and purpose in the direction of change, to rest their inquiries on grounds of what ought to be rather than what is happening. The machine has not reached those rooms yet. Whether it ever will, I cannot say. I only know what it looks like when it arrives.
