# Chapter X: Decay of Business Enterprise — Fables

---

**The Merchant and the Mill**

A merchant built a great mill on a river, and the mill ground grain faster than any hand could grind. The merchant grew rich from the mill's work. But the mill's wheel, turning day and night, shook the merchant's house, which stood on the same bank. Cracks ran through the walls. The merchant could not stop the wheel, for his wealth depended on it. Nor could he mend the walls faster than the wheel cracked them.

His neighbor asked, "Will you shut the mill down?"

"I cannot," said the merchant. "Without it I am nothing."

"Then will you move your house?"

"There is nowhere else to build on this river."

*The growth of business enterprise rests on the machine technology as its material foundation. The machine industry is indispensable to it; it cannot get along without the machine process. But the discipline of the machine process cuts away the spiritual, institutional foundations of business enterprise; it cannot, in the long run, get along with the machine process.*

---

**The Halfway Bridge**

A village built a bridge halfway across a gorge. It rested on a single pillar of mixed stone -- one kind of rock on the left, another on the right, bound together with mortar. For a generation, the bridge held. But rain dissolved the mortar, and the two kinds of stone shifted against each other. The villagers could not push the stones back into place, for the rain would not stop. Nor could they build the bridge again from scratch, for the old pillar's foundation was already crumbling.

A traveler passing through remarked, "Your bridge was never meant to last. It was the work of a dry season."

*The institutional basis of business enterprise -- the system of natural rights -- appears to be a peculiarly instable affair. There is no way of retaining it under changing circumstances, and there is no way of returning to it after circumstances have changed.*

---

**The Monks and the Leaking Roof**

Rain came through the monastery roof. The monks formed committees. One group sang hymns to lift morale. Another decorated the wet stones with garlands. A third organized lectures on the history of roofing. A fourth collected alms for the poor monks who sat beneath the worst leaks.

The rain continued. A traveling tinker asked if anyone had thought to mend the roof.

"We are not tinkers," said the abbot. "We are men of principle."

*These remedial measures whereby it is proposed to save or to rehabilitate certain praiseworthy but obsolescent habits of life and of thought are, all and several, beside the point. Nothing can serve as a corrective of the cultural trend given by the machine discipline except what can be put in the form of a business proposition.*

---

**The Fox and His Virtues**

A fox who kept a trading-post was known for three things: he always paid his debts, he saved his coins, and he never showed his hand in a deal. These qualities served him well. But a young fox asked him, "Are you also brave? Are you devout? Do you carry yourself with dignity?"

The old fox blinked. "I am solvent," he said. "I am thrifty. And I know when to hold my tongue. What else does a fox need?"

The young fox went away unsatisfied, for he had been hoping to learn something noble.

*What it conserves is the bourgeois virtues of solvency, thrift, and dissimulation. The nobler and more spectacular aristocratic virtues, with their correlative institutional furniture, are not in any sensible degree fortified by the habits of business life.*

---

**The Schoolmaster's Bargain**

A schoolmaster taught his pupils to read, to reason, and to wonder at the world. Then a rich man endowed the school and sat on its board. "What use is wonder?" asked the rich man. "Teach them something practical." So the schoolmaster taught the children to keep accounts and write receipts.

The children learned quickly. They could reckon any sum. But when a strange bird landed in the schoolyard, not one of them thought to ask what it was.

*"Practical" means useful for private gain. There is a progressive narrowing of instruction to such learning as is designed to give a ready application of results rather than a systematic organization of knowledge.*

---

**The Crier in the Market**

A town crier was hired by the merchants to announce the day's news. At first he called out what had happened. But the cloth merchant said, "Do not mention the moth in my woolens." The baker said, "Say nothing of weevils." The wine seller said, "If you must speak of vinegar, speak of it as a delicacy."

In time the crier's announcements bore little resemblance to the day's events. Yet the townsfolk listened and nodded, for the crier told them what they already wished to believe.

A stranger asked, "Does no one here know the truth?"

"The truth," said the crier, "is not my employment."

*The first duty of an editor is to gauge the sentiments of his readers, and then tell them what they like to believe. The net result is that both the news columns and the editorial columns are commonly meretricious in a high degree.*

---

**The Bees and the Magazine**

The bees of a prosperous hive produced a weekly gazette, paid for by the pollen merchants. The writing was elegant. The illustrations were fine. Every article praised the goodness of clover and the dignity of the queen.

A young bee, reading it, said, "This is very pleasant, but I notice it says nothing of substance."

An older bee replied, "It is not meant to say anything of substance. It is meant to carry the advertisements past your eyes. And that it does beautifully."

*The literary output issued under the surveillance of the advertising office is excellent in workmanship and deficient in intelligence and substantial originality. Harmlessness, not to say pointlessness, and an edifying, gossipy optimism are the substantial characteristics.*

**The King Who Called for War**

A kingdom of free craftsmen grew restless. They questioned the old customs. They doubted the king's right to his title. So the king declared war on a neighboring land.

The soldiers learned to obey without question. The citizens learned to cheer without thinking. The craftsmen stopped asking difficult questions and began speaking of honor, rank, and glory.

"Good," said the king. "Order is restored."

But a counselor whispered, "Sire, this is not the order you had before. The craftsmen once questioned your laws but obeyed them freely. Now they obey from fear and habit. You have not restored your old kingdom. You have built a different one -- and an older one."

*The warlike culture takes back to a more archaic situation that preceded the scheme of natural rights. It makes not for a reinstatement of the Natural Rights of Man but for a reversion to the Grace of God.*

---

**The Two Winds**

Two winds blew against a merchant's house, one from the east and one from the west. The east wind was the wind of the machine -- it wore away the walls. The west wind was the wind of war -- it blew the house back toward older, darker ground.

The merchant could not stop either wind. If the east wind prevailed, his house would crumble. If the west wind prevailed, his house would be carried back into a country where merchants did not rule.

He stood in the doorway, between the two winds, and understood that his tenancy was temporary.

*The full dominion of business enterprise is necessarily a transitory dominion. It stands to lose in the end whether the one or the other of the two divergent cultural tendencies wins, because it is incompatible with the ascendancy of either.*
