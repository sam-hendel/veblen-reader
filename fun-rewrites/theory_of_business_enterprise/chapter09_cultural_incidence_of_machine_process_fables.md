# Chapter IX: Cultural Incidence of Machine Process — Fables

---

**The Weaver and the Loom**

A weaver once worked at his own pace, choosing his threads by feel, pausing when the light pleased him, singing when the mood struck. Then the village acquired a great loom, powered by the river. The weaver was set to work at it. He could not pause when the light pleased him, for the shuttle flew at the river's pace. He could not choose threads by feel, for the loom demanded threads of standard gauge. When he tried once to improvise a flourish of his own, the fabric jammed and tore. After a season, the weaver found he could think of nothing but tension and timing, gauge and grade. His wife complained he had become dull. He had not become dull. He had become precise.

*The machine process compels the adaptation of the workman to his work, rather than the adaptation of the work to the workman.*

---

**The Merchant and the Millwright**

A merchant and a millwright sat arguing in a tavern. The merchant said: "This land belongs to the baron by ancient right, and it is just that he collects his rents." The millwright said: "The river turns the wheel whether the baron wills it or not. What has right to do with water pressure?" The merchant replied: "Without right, there is no order." The millwright replied: "Without pressure, there is no flour." They argued until closing and convinced each other of nothing.

*Arguments which proceed on material cause and effect cannot be met with arguments from conventional precedent or dialectically sufficient reason, and conversely.*

---

**The Fox Who Kept No Larder**

The fox who worked at the mill kept no larder. The badger, a farmer, scolded him for it each autumn. "You earn enough," said the badger. "Why do you save nothing?" The fox considered this. He did not own his den -- he moved wherever the mill needed him. He could not store grain, for he might be sent to another mill next season. And somehow the habit of storing had simply ceased to interest him. He spent what he had and moved on, light and unburdened. The badger shook his head and dug his cellar deeper.

*The conditions of life imposed upon the working population by the machine industry discourage thrift. The industrial classes appear to be losing the instinct of individual ownership.*

---

**The Monastery and the Smithy**

In a certain town, the monks preached the old doctrines, and the smiths worked the new forges. The monks spoke of sin, of obedience to a heavenly master, of the sacred order of things. The smiths nodded politely and went back to their hammers. They could not feel themselves sinners. Their work taught them only cause and effect: if you heat iron to the right temperature, it bends; if you do not, it breaks. There was no sin in a broken hinge, only an error in temperature. The abbot lamented that the smiths were losing their souls. The smiths did not notice.

*The machine throws out anthropomorphic habits of thought. The consolations of a personal relation of subservience to a supernatural master do not appeal to men whose habit of life is shaped by a familiarity with the relations of impersonal cause and effect.*

---

**The Two Castles**

Two castles stood on opposite banks of a river. In the first, the lord governed by ancient custom: his word was law because his father's word had been law. In the second, the engineers governed the waterworks: water flowed because the pipes were sound. Over the years, the lord's subjects began crossing the river to work at the waterworks. They returned home each evening a little less impressed by the lord's decrees. They did not rebel. They simply stopped believing. The lord's castle still stood, but the awe that had held it up was quietly draining away, like water through a cracked foundation.

*There results an ever weakening sense of conviction, allegiance, or piety toward the received institutions.*

---

**The Bees and Their Hive**

The bees of an old hive had always worked under the queen's charter, which set out each bee's portion and place. Then came a new method of building comb -- faster, more regular, requiring the bees to work in tighter coordination. The bees who worked the new method began to chafe at the charter. They did not propose a new charter. They did not demand a larger portion. They simply stopped caring about charters altogether. The drones who managed the hive's stores were baffled. "What do they want?" asked one drone. "They do not seem to want anything we can give them," said another. "They want to abolish wanting," said a third, and was laughed at, but was closest to the truth.

*It looks to the disappearance of property rights rather than their redistribution. No substantial agreement upon a point of knowledge or conviction is possible between persons who proceed from disparate preconceptions.*

---

**The Carpenter's Union**

The carpenters of a certain town agreed among themselves: no carpenter would work longer than ten hours, and no carpenter would work for less than a fixed wage. The magistrate declared this unlawful, for it violated every man's natural right to make his own bargain. The carpenters did not argue with the magistrate. They simply continued as before, outside his court. Their arrangements were not written in the language of rights. They were written in the language of standard measures and mutual necessity. The magistrate was correct in his law. The carpenters were correct in their practice. Neither could make the other see it.

*Trade-union action is at variance with the natural-rights foundation of the common law. The regulations violate that system of law and order which rests on natural rights, although they may be enforced by that de facto law and order which is embodied in the mechanical standardization of the industrial processes.*

**The Conservative and the Iconoclast**

Two brothers inherited their father's opinions. The elder, a banker, kept them all. The younger, a machinist, let most of them go. By the end of their lives, the elder still believed what their father had believed, and the younger believed almost none of it. Yet a stranger visiting each brother in turn would have found them more alike than different -- for even the elder had quietly dropped a few of his father's convictions, and the younger had kept a few without noticing. They were not moving in opposite directions. One was simply moving faster than the other.

*The discrepancy between the conservatives and the iconoclasts need not be taken to mean that the two contrasted classes are moving in opposite directions. Taken generally, both wings have been moving in the direction of a more impersonal, more matter-of-fact, less conventional point of view.*

---

**The Craftsman and the Philosopher**

A philosopher once asked a craftsman, "What is the chief end of man?" The craftsman, who spent his days adjusting gears and measuring tolerances, thought about it for a long time. "I don't know about ends," he said at last. "I only know about processes." The philosopher was appalled. But the craftsman was not being evasive. His daily work had taught him to attend to how things happen, not why they were meant to happen. He could describe the sequence of any mechanism in his shop with perfect clarity. He could not say what any of it was for.

*The characteristically modern science does not inquire about prime causes, design in nature, desirability of effects, ultimate results, or eschatological consequences.*

---

**The Leveller**

A king, a priest, a merchant, and a smith all lived in the same town. The king ruled by lineage, the priest by scripture, the merchant by contract, and the smith by the logic of his forge. Over the years, the forge grew larger, and its rhythms crept into everything. The king found his subjects less awed. The priest found his flock less devout. The merchant found his contracts less sacred. Only the smith noticed nothing, for to him nothing had changed -- the iron still behaved as iron behaves. The others called him a vulgarizer. He called himself busy.

*The machine is a leveller, a vulgarizer, whose end seems to be the extirpation of all that is respectable, noble, and dignified in human intercourse and ideals.*
