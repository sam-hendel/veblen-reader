# Chapter II: The Machine Process — Fables

---

**The Chemist and the Blacksmith**

A blacksmith boasted that the machine process belonged to him, for he had the largest hammer and the most intricate bellows in the village. A chemist who lived next door smiled and said nothing. One day the blacksmith's finest ore would not yield good steel, no matter how he hammered it. The chemist examined the ore, adjusted the heat and the mixture of charcoal andite, and drew out a perfect blade. "But you have no machine," the blacksmith protested. The chemist shrugged. "Your hammer reshapes the metal, but the metal also reshapes itself -- by the help of the apparatus."

*It is a question of the character of the process rather than a question of the complexity of the contrivances employed.*

---

**The Monks and the Canal**

Seven monasteries sat along a single canal. Each drew water for its mill, and each returned the water for the next. For years the system ran well, each monastery minding its wheel. Then the third monastery, wanting a finer grade of flour, narrowed its sluice. The fourth monastery's wheel slowed. The fifth stopped altogether. By evening, the seventh monastery had no bread. The abbot of the first monastery walked the length of the canal and declared: no wheel turns alone.

*No one of the mechanical processes carried on by the use of a given outfit of appliances is independent of other processes going on elsewhere.*

---

**The Carpenter's Odd Nail**

A carpenter fashioned his own nails, each one shaped by hand and slightly different from the last. He was proud of his craft. But when a great bridge was to be built, and a thousand carpenters were called together, his nails would not fit the joints cut by other men. His beams could not receive the bolts forged in the city. He sat idle while the others worked. No one mocked him; they simply could not use what he had brought.

*Irregularity, departure from standard measurements in any of the measurable facts, is of itself a fault in any item that is to find a use in the industrial process, for it brings delay.*

---

**The Fisherman and the Schedule**

A fisherman on a great river caught his fish when the fish were willing and brought them to market when his boat was full. But the new ferry ran on a schedule. The new ice-house opened and closed at fixed hours. The new market rang a bell at dawn and shut its gates at noon. The fisherman found that if he did not arrive with the bell, his fish rotted; if he did not match the ice-house hours, he had no ice; if he missed the ferry, he could not cross. Slowly, without anyone commanding him, his whole day arranged itself around the schedule.

*Throughout the scheme of life the industrial process makes itself felt and enforces a degree of conformity to the canon of accurate quantitative measurement.*

---

**The Weavers and the Dyed Wool**

In a town of weavers, each household once dyed its wool in its own way -- one with walnut, one with madder, one with indigo mixed to private taste. The cloth that came from each loom was unlike any other. Then the great looms came, and with them, wool dyed to standard grades: three reds, four blues, two greens. The housewives grumbled, but the drapers stocked only what the looms required. Within a generation, every home was furnished in the standard colors. A grandmother remembered the old walnut brown. Her granddaughter had never seen it.

*The idiosyncrasies of the individual consumers are required to conform to the uniform gradations imposed upon consumable goods by the comprehensive mechanical processes of industry.*

---

**The Potter's Cracked Kiln**

A potter's kiln cracked, and he could fire no clay. This was a small matter -- one kiln among many. But the tile-maker who bought his tiles could not roof the warehouse. The grain merchant whose warehouse stood open lost his grain to rain. The baker who bought the merchant's grain found none to buy. The children of the town went without bread, all because of a cracked kiln. The elders marveled that so small a fault could travel so far.

*A disturbance at any point, whereby any given branch of industry fails to do its share in the work of the system at large, immediately affects the neighbouring or related branches, and is transmitted through their derangement to the remoter portions of the system.*

---

**The Merchant at the Lock-Gate**

Seven mills drew power from the same river. The water flowed through a system of locks, and someone had to adjust the gates -- opening here, closing there -- so that each mill received its share. This work fell to a merchant, who owned no mill and ground no grain, but who held the contracts for the water. When two mills needed more flow at the same hour, it was the merchant who decided which would run and which would wait. He produced nothing, but the whole arrangement turned on his word.

*It therefore rests with the business men to make or mar the running adjustments of industry.*
