# Chapter VIII: Business Principles in Law and Politics — Fables

---

**The Wolves and the Sheep Pen**

The wolves and the sheep lived together in a great valley. When it came time to choose who would manage the common pasture, the wolves stepped forward, for they were the most active creatures in the valley and understood the movement of herds better than anyone. The sheep agreed, for it seemed obvious that whoever managed herds best should manage the pasture. The wolves arranged everything to suit their hunting, and the sheep walked the paths the wolves laid out, and when a sheep complained, the others shushed him, because the wolves were, after all, the experts on herds.

*Modern politics is business politics. The management of the affairs of the community at large falls by common consent into the hands of business men and is guided by business considerations.*

---

**The Old Fairground**

Once there was a fairground where any craftsman could set up a stall. A potter competed with a potter, a weaver with a weaver, and each stood or fell by his own dexterity and diligence. The rules of the fair were simple: no man may block another man's stall, and every man is free to sell as he pleases. These rules were carved in stone at the fairground gate, and they were good rules, for the craftsmen were roughly equal in their means, and the fair was loose-jointed enough that a clever newcomer could always find a place.

Then one day great mills were built at the edge of the fair, and the craftsmen's stalls stood empty. But the rules carved in stone remained. They had been made for a world that no longer existed.

*The scheme of natural rights grew up under the discipline of the small industry and petty trade, whose development culminated in the eighteenth century. In industrial matters the efficient and autonomous factor was the individual workman, his personal force, dexterity, and diligence.*

---

**The Invisible Chain**

A monk and a blacksmith argued before a judge. The blacksmith had forged a single great chain that ran through every workshop in the village, linking all the looms and all the bellows and all the mills together, so that none could work unless the chain turned. Then the blacksmith stopped the chain and told the weavers they must pay double for its turning.

The weavers brought their complaint to the judge. "He has bound us," they said. "We cannot work without his chain."

The judge examined the weavers carefully. "Has he struck you?" he asked. "No." "Has he entered your homes?" "No." "Has he broken any contract?" "No." The judge folded his hands. "Then he has done you no wrong. You are free to work or not work, as you please."

The weavers looked at the still chain and at their dark, silent workshops.

*Such coercion as the machine process may exert is, in point of legal reality, no coercion. It is, perhaps, actual, with a gross, material actuality; but it is not real, with a legal, metaphysically competent reality.*

---

**The Fish and the River**

A fish who lived in a river was told by the river's owner that he must pay half his food to swim there, or else leave. "But there is no other river," said the fish. "You are free to go," said the owner. "I do not compel you. The agreement between us is freely made." And it was true that the owner had neither struck the fish, nor seized him, nor blocked his path. The fish could leave the river at any time. There was simply nowhere else to swim.

*A given workman's livelihood can perhaps, practically, be found only on acceptance of one specific contract offered, perhaps not at all. But the coercion which bears upon his choice through the standardization of industrial procedure is neither assault and battery nor breach of contract, and it is, therefore, not repugnant to the principles of natural liberty.*

**The Learned Judges and the Jury of Sparrows**

A hawk had seized the nesting tree of a family of sparrows and would not let them return unless they sang for him from dawn to dusk. The sparrows brought their case before a jury of common birds, who ruled at once that the hawk must share the tree. But the hawk appealed to the High Court of Owls, who were learned in the ancient law.

The eldest owl adjusted his spectacles. "The hawk owns the tree," he said. "The sparrows are free to nest elsewhere. There is no violation of liberty here." The younger owls nodded, for they had studied the law longer than the jury birds and grasped its principles more firmly.

The common birds grumbled, but their grasp of legal principles was at fault.

*The higher courts, who are presumably in more intimate touch with the principles of jurisprudence, speak more unequivocally for the metaphysical principles. The juries, in great measure, speak for the strained sympathies of the vulgar, which are a matter somewhat apart from the foundations of law and order.*

**The King Who Became a Shopkeeper**

A king once ruled his lands for glory. He waged wars for honor, built monuments to his name, and taxed his subjects to fund the splendor of his court. Then the merchants of his kingdom grew powerful and said: "We will vote on the royal budget." The king agreed, for he needed their money.

Year by year, the merchants approved funds for roads that served their trade and denied funds for monuments that did not. The king still sat on his throne and wore his crown, but he waged wars where the merchants saw profit and kept peace where they saw cost. In time, no one could say where the king's policy ended and the merchants' business began.

*With the transition to constitutional rule, the discretion and autonomy in the case has passed from the hands of the prince into those of the business men, and the interests of the business men have superseded those of the crown.*

**The Goats on the Bridge**

All the goats in the valley crossed a single bridge to reach their pasture. The bridge belonged to a rich old billy goat, who one day decided to close it, for the price of grass on the far side had fallen and he saw no profit in keeping the bridge open. The other goats milled about on the near bank, growing hungry.

"Surely someone will stop him," said a kid. But the old goat had broken no law. The bridge was his. A few goats suggested that the common need for pasture ought to count for something, but they could not say exactly how, for no one had a right to another goat's bridge.

*The exercise of the rights of ownership in the interests of business may traverse the de facto necessities of a group or class; it may even traverse the needs of the community at large. But since these necessities cannot be formulated in terms of the natural freedom of contract, they can give rise to no cognizable grievance and find no legal remedy.*

---

**The Farmer and the Merchant**

A farmer believed that whatever was good for the merchant in his town was good for the farm. When the merchant prospered, the farmer prospered -- or so it seemed, for the merchant bought his grain. So the farmer voted as the merchant voted, and supported the rules the merchant favored, and when someone asked why, the farmer said it was simply obvious that their interests were the same.

He did not examine whether this was so. It was an article of faith, held most firmly by those who had the most to lose by doubting it.

*There is a naive, unquestioning persuasion abroad among the body of the people to the effect that, in some occult way, the material interests of the populace coincide with the pecuniary interests of those business men who live within the scope of the same set of governmental contrivances.*

---

**The Self-Made Crab**

On a rocky shore where there were no old shells to inherit and no elder crabs to copy, a young crab learned to survive entirely by his own claws. He scuttled, he hoarded, he traded shells with other crabs, and he measured everything -- food, shelter, safety, friendship -- by what it could be exchanged for. In time he could not imagine any other way of reckoning the world. He was entirely his own creation, and he was entirely a creature of exchange.

*America is the native habitat of the self-made man, and the self-made man is a pecuniary organism.*
