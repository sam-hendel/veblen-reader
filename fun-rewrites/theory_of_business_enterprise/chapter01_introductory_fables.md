# Chapter I: Introductory — Fables

---

**The Mill and the Village**

A village had many workers: a weaver, a baker, a shepherd, a woman who kept bees. Each went about their trade in their own way, at their own pace. Then a miller built a great water-mill at the river's bend. The mill ground flour faster than any hand-stone, and soon the baker depended on it, and the shepherd traded wool to buy its flour, and even the beekeeper found her days shaped by the rhythm of the mill's wheel. The weaver alone tried to hold apart, but her customers now kept the mill's hours, and she had no choice but to keep them too.

A traveler passing through asked who ruled the village. "No one," said the weaver, bitterly. "The mill does."

*The machine industries are in a dominant position; they set the pace for the rest of the industrial system.*

---

**The Ox and the Merchant**

An ox pulled a plow from dawn to dusk, turning the earth so that grain would grow. A merchant watched from the road. He did not plow, and he did not sow. But he owned the field, and he owned the plow, and when the harvest came, he decided where the grain would go and at what price. The ox said to a passing crow, "I do the work, yet he decides everything." The crow replied, "Does he hold your harness?" The ox said, "No. He holds the field."

*Through the mechanism of investments and markets, the business man controls the plants and processes, and these set the pace and determine the direction of movement for the rest.*

---

**The Bees and the Beekeeper's Wife**

A beekeeper's wife tended the hives every morning. She smoked the bees, pulled the frames, strained the honey, and sealed it in jars. None of this was done for profit; it was done because the household needed honey, and because she knew how. A tax collector came by and asked to see her ledgers. She had none. He asked for her terms of trade. She had none. He frowned and moved on, finding nothing to count.

Yet when the merchant in town raised the price of sugar, she found herself working the hives harder, and when the merchant lowered it, she let a few frames go unworked. She never spoke to the merchant. She never needed to.

*Those elements in the industrial world that take the initiative and exert a far-reaching coercive guidance in matters of industry go to their work with a view to profits on investment.*

---

**The River King**

A king ruled a valley but kept no army and issued no decrees. Instead, he owned the river. He decided when the sluice gates opened and closed. When he opened them, the farms below were watered and the mills could turn. When he closed them, the fields dried and the wheels stood still. No farmer was his servant. No miller owed him fealty. Each was free to come and go. But every one of them lived by the river, and the river was his.

A philosopher visited and asked the farmers if they were free men. "Entirely free," they said. "We do as we please." The philosopher then asked why they all planted the same crop at the same time. They looked at one another and had no answer.

*His control of the motions of other men is not strict, for they are not under coercion from him except through the coercion exercised by the exigencies of the situation in which their lives are cast; but as near as it may be said of any human power in modern times, the large business man controls the exigencies of life under which the community lives.*

---

**The Skeleton and the Spirit**

Two monks argued about the nature of their monastery. One said, "The monastery is its buildings, its vineyard, its scriptorium -- the things we can touch." The other said, "The monastery is its Rule, its daily offices, its purpose -- the spirit that moves through it." They went to the abbot. He listened, then said: "Tear down the buildings and keep the Rule, and in a year you will have new buildings. Keep the buildings and lose the Rule, and in a year you will have a ruin."

The first monk asked, "Then the spirit matters more?" The abbot shook his head. "The spirit without the skeleton is a ghost. The skeleton without the spirit is a corpse. You are asking me which blade of the scissors cuts."

*The material framework of modern civilization is the industrial system, and the directing force which animates this framework is business enterprise.*
