# Chapter V: Value of Loan Credit — Fables

---

**The Fishermen and the Borrowed Nets**

Ten fishermen worked the same lake, each with one net. They caught enough to live. Then one fisherman borrowed a second net from a moneylender and began pulling in twice the catch. Seeing this, the others all borrowed nets too. Soon every fisherman had two nets in the water. But the lake held only so many fish. Within a season each man was catching about what he had caught before -- only now every one of them owed the moneylender for a net.

The fisherman who had started it said to his wife, "If I give up the borrowed net, I will fall behind the others." She said, "And if you keep it?" He said, "I break even, less what I owe."

*Borrowed funds afford any given business concern a differential advantage as against other competitors; but it is, in the main, a differential advantage only.*

---

**The Monks and the Second Ledger**

A monastery kept two ledgers. The first recorded its real property: its fields, its sheep, its mill, its store of grain. The second recorded its debts and credits -- what it owed to lenders, what it had borrowed against the value of its fields. A visiting bishop examined both books and said, "Your second ledger claims you are twice as wealthy as your first." The abbot nodded. "The lenders count our fields once as property and again as the basis for what they have lent us. On paper we have two mills. In truth we have one."

The bishop asked, "Then what is the second mill?" The abbot said, "A number."

*To a very considerable extent the funds involved in these loans have only a pecuniary existence, not a material one; and, so far as that is true, they represent, in the aggregate, only fictitious industrial equipment.*

---

**The Rising Price of Barley**

A grain merchant owned a warehouse of barley worth a hundred coins. He pledged the barley to borrow fifty coins, which he used to buy more barley, raising its price. Now his warehouse was valued at a hundred and twenty coins. On this higher valuation he borrowed again, bought more, and the price rose further. Each rise in price gave him new collateral, and each new loan drove prices higher still. His neighbors marveled at his wealth.

Then a dry summer came and the bakers could not afford bread at such prices. They bought less. The price of barley dropped. The merchant's collateral shrank, his lenders called their loans, and the barley that had been valued at two hundred coins sold for eighty.

*The extension of credit proceeds on the putative stability of the money value of the capitalized industrial material, whose money value is cumulatively augmented by this extension itself.*

---

**The Creditor's Harvest**

A farmer owned good land and a plow. To expand his planting, he borrowed from a townsman, pledging his land as security. For several years he planted more, earned more, and borrowed more against the rising value of his fields. The townsman had never held a plow. He had never sown a seed. He simply lent money and collected interest.

Then the harvest failed and the price of grain collapsed. The farmer could not repay. The townsman took the land, the plow, and the barn. He had produced nothing, but he now owned everything.

A neighbor who witnessed this asked who had gained from all those years of borrowing. The answer was plain enough.

*Apart from secondary effects, the main final outcome is a redistribution of the ownership of property whereby the creditor class, including holders and claimants of funds, is benefited.*

**The Two Coopers**

Two coopers made barrels in the same town. The first worked steadily with his own tools and timber. The second borrowed money to buy faster lathes and twice the timber, and turned out barrels at a great rate. For a time the second cooper prospered and the first barely held on. But the second owed interest on every loan, and the faster he worked, the more he needed to borrow to keep ahead.

A visitor asked the first cooper why he did not borrow as his rival did. "If I do not borrow," said the cooper, "I cannot earn a reasonable living, because he has driven prices down. But if I do borrow, I must run twice as fast to stand in the same place."

*Under modern conditions business cannot profitably be done by any one of the competitors without the customary resort to credit. Without the customary resort to credit a reasonable return could not be obtained on the investment.*

---

**The Cartographer's Castle**

A king wished to appear powerful. He summoned his cartographer and said, "Draw my castle twice as large on every map." The cartographer did so. Foreign princes, seeing the maps, sent tribute. The king's ministers, consulting the maps, believed the treasury must be vast, and spent accordingly. For a time, the kingdom seemed to thrive.

Then an enemy army arrived and found the castle was exactly the size it had always been. The walls were no thicker for having been drawn thicker. The moats were no deeper.

*The buoyancy which a speculative inflation of values gives to industrial business may indirectly increase the material output of industry by enhancing the intensity with which the industrial process is carried on under the added stimulus; but apart from this psychological effect the expansion of business capital through credit extension has no aggregate industrial effect.*

---

**The Promoter's Feast**

Three bakers each owned a small bakery. A promoter came to them and said, "Merge your three shops into one great bakery, and you shall be richer for it." He combined their ovens under a single name, issued shares worth far more than the three shops together -- for the shares included the good name and reputation of each baker, and a further good name imputed to the new combined concern. The promoter took a large share of stock as his fee. So did the moneylender who financed the arrangement.

When it was done, the ovens were the same three ovens. The flour was the same flour. But the paper said the bakery was worth five times what the three small shops had been worth. The promoter had already sold his shares and moved on to the next town.

*The trust-maker is in some respects a surrogate for a commercial crisis.*

---

**The Name on the Door**

A blacksmith forged excellent hinges and was known throughout the county. A merchant offered to buy his shop -- not the anvil, not the hammer, not the iron stock, but the name on the door. "Your name is worth more than your tools," said the merchant. The blacksmith was puzzled. "My name makes nothing. It bends no iron." The merchant smiled. "Your name is what lets me charge twice the price."

He bought the name, hired a cheaper smith, and issued bonds against the expected earnings that the name would bring. The bonds exceeded the value of every physical thing in the shop. When the cheaper smith proved less skilled and customers drifted away, the bonds still stood, demanding their payment.

*Good-will is the nucleus of capitalization in modern corporation finance.*

---

**The Ship That Was Pledged Twice**

A shipwright built a fine vessel. He pledged it to one lender to borrow money for cargo. He then pledged the same vessel -- now laden with cargo bought on credit -- to a second lender for still more money. Both lenders believed they held a claim on a ship. But there was only one ship. When a storm sank it, two men came to collect one hull, and found nothing but water.

An old sailor on the dock shook his head. "You cannot sail a loan," he said. "And you cannot drown one either. That is the trouble."

*Funds of whatever character are a pecuniary fact, not an industrial one; they serve the distribution of the control of industry only, not its materially productive work.*
