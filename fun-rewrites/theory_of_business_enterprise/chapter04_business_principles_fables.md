# Chapter IV: Business Principles --- Fables

---

**The Potter and the Priest**

A potter shaped bowls all day long and sold them at market. A priest, passing by, said: "By what authority do you claim these bowls as yours?" The potter stared. "I made them," he said. "My hands shaped the clay." The priest shook his head. "In former times, a man held his goods by grant of his lord, who held them by grant of the king, who held them by grant of God. You have no grant." The potter sold another bowl. The priest went home to an empty cupboard.

*The question then pressing for an answer when a point of legitimacy was raised was not, What hath God wrought? but, What hath God ordained? But the free workman, master of his own motions, had become so habitual a fact that free labor was accepted uncritically as the fundamental factor in all human economy.*

---

**The Monkey and the Old Chains**

A monkey had once worn a collar of clan loyalty, a chain of divine right, and a leash of feudal allegiance. Over the years these had rusted and snapped, one by one, until the monkey moved freely. But around its waist it still wore a bright belt marked "PROPERTY," and this belt it polished every morning. The other monkeys asked: "Why do you keep that one?" The monkey looked puzzled. "What belt?" it said.

*While these other conventional norms are in their decline, this younger one of the inherited institutions stands forth without apology and shows no apprehension of being crowded into the background of sentimental reminiscence.*

---

**The Merchant's Ruler**

A merchant had a wooden ruler he used to measure cloth. Over the years the ruler warped and shrank, so that what he called "one yard" was sometimes longer and sometimes shorter. But he kept his accounts in yards, and when the cloth he bought seemed to grow or shrink, he marked the difference down as a change in the cloth, never in the ruler. His customers complained. The merchant showed them his books. "The yard is the yard," he said. "It is the cloth that varies."

*Usage fortified by law decides that when prices vary the variation is held to occur in the value of the vendible commodities, not in the value of the money unit, since money is the standard of value.*

---

**The Fisherman Who Expected the Sea to Rise**

A fisherman kept his boat in a harbor. Each season, when fish were plentiful, the water seemed to rise and his boat floated higher. He came to expect this. "It is natural," he told the other fishermen, "for the harbor to rise a little each year." When the water dropped one season, he was outraged. "Something has gone wrong," he said. "The harbor is broken." The other fishermen pointed out that harbors do not rise. But the fisherman had already written "rising harbor" into his charts, and he would not scratch it out.

*Gain, they feel, is normal, being the purpose of all their endeavors; whereas a loss or a shrinkage in the values invested is felt to be an untoward accident which does not belong in the normal course of business, and which requires particular explanation.*

---

**The Two Abbots**

Two abbots each ran a monastery. The first abbot counted his wealth by the size of his granary, the strength of his walls, and the number of his sheep. The second abbot counted his wealth by what his monastery could earn in a year, multiplied by a number he kept in his head. When a storm destroyed half the first abbot's granary, he said: "I have lost half my grain." When the same storm scattered the second abbot's sheep, he said: "My earning-capacity has declined by a sixth, so I have lost a sixth of my wealth." The sheep came back. The grain did not.

*The question of capital in business has increasingly become a question of capitalization on the basis of earning-capacity, rather than a question of the magnitude of the industrial plant or the cost of production of the appliances of industry.*

---

**The Cobbler and the Factory**

A cobbler made shoes. Each pair bore the mark of his hand, and when he sold them, no one questioned that they were his to sell---he had made them. Then a factory was built. A hundred men worked inside, none of them making a whole shoe, each repeating one motion all day. The factory owner, who had never touched leather, sold the shoes. A child asked the cobbler: "Did that man make all those shoes?" The cobbler looked at the factory and said nothing, for the old answer no longer fit, but no one had furnished a new one.

*The workman's force, ingenuity, and dexterity was the ultimate economic factor---ultimate in a manner patent to the common sense of a generation habituated to the system of handicraft, however doubtful such a view may appear in the eyes of a generation in whose apprehension the workman is no longer the prime mover nor the sole, or even chief, efficient factor in the industrial process.*

---

**The Monks Who Forgot Why They Bowed**

In a mountain monastery, monks bowed three times before entering the kitchen. No one remembered why. One young monk asked the eldest brother. "It is how things are done," the eldest said. "But why?" asked the young monk. The eldest thought for a long while. "Perhaps," he said, "the ceiling was once very low." The ceiling was not low. The monks kept bowing.

*Any theory that gains widespread and uncritical acceptance must carry a competent formulation of the deliverances of common sense on the matter with which it deals. Otherwise it will not be generally accepted. And such a commonplace view is in its turn an outcome of protracted experience on the part of the community.*

---

**The Geese and the Cash Payment**

A flock of geese had always settled disputes by combat. The strongest goose won. Then a clever goose proposed that instead of fighting, any grievance could be settled by a payment of seeds. The geese agreed. Soon every old custom---debts of loyalty, obligations of kinship, duties to the flock---was converted into a quantity of seeds. A mother goose owed her goslings twelve seeds for raising them. A sentry goose was paid four seeds per watch. An elder goose observed that things had been simpler when debts were not all of one kind. But the seeds were easy to count, and counting was easier than remembering.

*The pecuniary norm has invaded the domain of the older institutions, such as blood-relationship, citizenship, or the church, so that obligations belonging under the one or the other of these may now be assessed and fulfilled in terms of a money payment, although the notion of a pecuniary liquidation seems to have been wholly remote from the range of ideas on which these relations and duties were originally based.*

---

**The Moneylender's Quiet Victory**

In a market town, the moneylender was once reviled. Merchants spat when he passed. Priests denounced him from the pulpit. Borrowers paid him in secret and denied it in public. But the town grew, and trade grew with it, and more and more people needed coin before they had earned it. After a generation, the moneylender's grandson sat on the town council. After two generations, his great-granddaughter was married to the mayor. No one spat anymore. The pulpit was silent on the matter. The interest rate was posted on the wall of the town hall, next to the price of bread.

*Only under more settled commercial relations during the era of handicraft did the payment of interest gradually come to be accepted into full legitimacy.*

---

**The King Who Became a Shopkeeper**

A king ruled his lands by decree. His nobles held their estates by his pleasure, and his peasants held their strips by the pleasure of the nobles. Then merchants came, and with them came contracts, and with contracts came the habit of asking not "Who granted this?" but "Who made this?" and "What is it worth?" The king found that his decrees mattered less each year. One day a merchant offered to buy the king's castle. The king was insulted. His grandson sold it.

*New axioms of right and truth supplant the old as new habits of thought supersede the old.*
