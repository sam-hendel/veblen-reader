# Chapter III: Business Enterprise -- Fables

---

**The Merchant and the Mill**

A merchant bought a flour mill. He knew nothing of grinding wheat, but he knew a great deal about the price of flour. When the millstones wore thin and the miller begged for new ones, the merchant said, "Not yet -- flour is cheap this month. We will wait." When flour was dear again, he ordered the mill to run day and night until the axle cracked. The miller wept over his broken machinery. The merchant studied his ledger and was satisfied.

*Industry is carried on for the sake of business, and not conversely; and the progress and activity of industry are conditioned by the outlook of the market, which means the presumptive chance of business profits.*

---

**The Fox and the River**

A fox discovered that when he rolled boulders into the river, the fish downstream were stunned and easy to catch. So he rolled boulders every morning. Some days he caught many fish. Other days the boulders merely muddied the water and the fish downstream starved. A heron asked, "Do you not care whether the river runs well or badly?" The fox said, "I care whether there are stunned fish floating to the surface."

*To the business man who aims at a differential gain arising out of interstitial adjustments or disturbances of the industrial system, it is not a material question whether his operations have an immediate furthering or hindering effect upon the system at large. The end is pecuniary gain, the means is disturbance of the industrial system.*

---

**The Weavers and the Wool-Buyer**

Three weavers in neighboring villages each made fine cloth. A wool-buyer saw that if the three looms worked together under one roof, the cloth would be better and cheaper. But each weaver wanted to be master. The wool-buyer set them against one another for two years, spoiling their trade and souring their customers, until at last they were too poor to refuse his terms. He built his single workshop and was celebrated as a great organizer of the craft.

*Business interests and manoeuvres commonly delay consolidations, combinations, correlations of the several plants and processes, for some appreciable time after such measures have become patently advisable on industrial grounds.*

---

**The Captain of the Monks**

A monastery had twelve monks copying manuscripts. Each monk kept his own ink, his own parchment, and his own accounts with the abbot. They spent half their waking hours haggling with one another over the cost of shared candles and desk-space. Then a new abbot arrived and put all supplies under one hand. Six monks were dismissed, and the remaining six produced more manuscripts than the twelve had done. The new abbot was praised as a great reformer. But what he had chiefly reformed was the excess of abbots.

*The heroic role of the captain of industry is that of a deliverer from an excess of business management. It is a casting out of business men by the chief of business men.*

---

**The Inventor and the Merchant**

A blacksmith devised a new bellows that doubled the heat of his forge. He showed it to the iron merchant who owned the forge. The merchant looked at it, nodded, and locked it in a chest. "When the price of iron is right," he said, "we will use it." Three years later, when a rival threatened his trade, the merchant brought out the bellows and was praised for his vision. The blacksmith, who had been stoking the old bellows all that time, said nothing.

*The men in industry must first create the mechanical possibility of such new and more efficient methods, before the business man sees the chance, makes the necessary business arrangements, and gives general directions that the contemplated industrial advance shall go into effect. The period between the time of earliest practicability and the effectual completion marks the interval by which the business man retards the advance of industry.*

---

**The Two Bakers**

Two bakers set up shop on the same street. Their bread was the same. Each spent more and more on painted signs, on boys to shout in the square, on ribbons tied to their loaves. In time each baker spent more on the shouting boys than on flour. A customer asked why the bread cost so much. "The cost of production," said the first baker gravely. The second baker nodded.

*Competitive advertising is an unavoidable item in the aggregate costs of industry. It does not add to the serviceability of the output. What it aims at is the sale of the output, and it is for this purpose that it is useful. It gives vendibility, which is useful to the seller, but has no utility to the last buyer.*

---

**The Fishmonger Who Sold to Strangers**

A fishmonger in a small village was known to every family. He dared not sell a rotten fish, for the buyer's children played with his children. Then he came into a great fortune and opened a vast fish-hall in the city, selling to thousands he would never meet. He found that a certain admixture of bad fish among the good was entirely profitable, and troubled his conscience not at all.

*One can with an easier conscience and with less of a sense of meanness take advantage of the necessities of people whom one knows of only as an indiscriminate aggregate of consumers.*

---

**The Prosperous Parasite**

A vine grew on an oak tree. It bore no acorns and gave no shade, but it grew thick and green by drawing sap from the oak's roots. A woodpecker observed that the oak was still mighty enough to bear the vine and a dozen like it. "True," said an old crow, "but the oak works harder than it otherwise would, merely to stay alive."

*While it is in the nature of things unavoidable that the management of industry by modern business methods should involve a large misdirection of effort and a very large waste of goods and services, these pecuniary aims and ideals have a very great effect in making men work hard and unremittingly, so that the business system probably compensates for any wastes involved in its working. It makes up for its wastefulness by the added strain which it throws upon those engaged in the productive work.*
