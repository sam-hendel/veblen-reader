# Chapter VII: Theory of Modern Welfare --- Fables

---

**The Two Famines**

In the old days, when the rains failed, the village starved because there was not enough grain. The baker had no flour; the miller had no wheat; the fields were bare.

In the new village, the granaries were full. The fields had yielded more than ever. But the merchant who owned the grain could not sell it at a price that covered what he had paid, and so the granary doors stayed shut. The baker had no flour. The children went hungry, same as before.

A traveler, passing through, asked why the grain sat locked away while people starved. The merchant shrugged.

*Under the old regime, dearth meant privation and might mean famine; under the new regime, low prices commonly mean privation and may on occasion mean famine.*

---

**The Rising Tide**

A school of fish discovered a warm current. The first fish to find it grew fat on the insects it carried. Seeing this, the other fish rushed in. For a time, every fish in the river was swimming faster and eating more.

But the warm current had stirred up all the insects at once. When they were gone, the river was emptier than before. The fish had grown used to eating well and had taken on obligations -- nests built in expensive gravel, debts owed to the crayfish. Now the food was gone, but the debts remained.

The oldest fish observed that this always happened. The warm current always came and always went, and the debts always outlasted the food.

*An era of prosperity is an era of rising prices. When prices cease to rise, prosperity is on the wane.*

---

**The Wages of the Ant**

During the boom at the riverside, the ants were hired to carry more loads than usual. They were not paid more per load, but they were grateful for the extra work. "Full employment!" they said. "We are prospering!"

Gradually the price of seeds rose, and the ants found their wages bought less than before. By the time their wages finally rose to match, the boom was ending. The queen beetle who employed them sighed: the higher wages were cutting into her margins.

"Your raise," she told the ants, "is what killed the good times."

*An era of prosperity does not commonly bring an increase of wages until the era is about to close. The advance of wages is not only a symptom indicating that the season of prosperity is passing, but it is a business factor which must by its own proper effect close the season of prosperity.*

---

**The Monastery Ledger**

A monastery kept bees. The monks improved their hives each year -- better frames, better smokers, better technique. Each year they produced more honey at less effort.

But the abbot kept his books in coins, not in jars of honey. The monastery's equipment, bought at last year's prices, was always worth less than what the ledger said, because a new monastery could now buy better hives for fewer coins. The gap between what the books claimed and what the hives actually earned never closed. Each improvement in beekeeping made the abbot's ledger a little more fictional.

He could not bring himself to write down the value of his hives. A reduction of the pecuniary showing is submitted to only reluctantly, even when it carries no hardship in the way of a reduced command over the material means of life.

*Under conditions which involve the requirement of a progressive rerating of this kind, this reluctant concession never overtakes the need of readjustment -- and the discrepancy between capitalization and earning-capacity is therefore chronic.*

---

**The Woodcutter's Malady**

A woodcutter owned a fine axe. He had paid ten silver pieces for it. Each year, the smiths made better axes for less money. A new axe, just as good as his, now cost only six silver pieces. His old axe still cut wood perfectly well. His pile of lumber was as high as ever.

But he felt poor. His axe was worth less than he had paid. His ledger showed a loss. He sat on his stump and refused to cut, because the price of lumber would not cover what he felt his axe was worth.

His neighbor, who had no axe at all, shivered in an unheated house and wondered why the woodcutter had stopped working when there was plenty of wood to cut.

*Depression is primarily a malady of the affections of the business men. That is the seat of the difficulty. The stagnation of industry and the hardships suffered by the workmen and other classes are of the nature of symptoms and secondary effects.*

---

**The Cooks Who Merged**

In a town with seven cooks, each tried to undercut the others. Prices fell until none of them could earn a living, though the townsfolk ate better than ever.

So the cooks combined into one kitchen. They fixed prices at what the traffic would bear. They produced less food, but at steady prices. Their ledgers recovered. The improvements each cook had made in the art of cooking -- the better ovens, the sharper knives -- no longer cheapened the price of a meal. The gains of efficiency accrued to the cooks' collective treasury rather than being passed to the diners.

A diner asked whether the merger had made anyone's supper better. It had not. The practical outcome appeared to be that material advantage inured to no one from the improvements, in so far as the combination successfully carried its point.

*The effect of such a coalition, in the case of ideal success, is to neutralize the cheapening of goods and services effected by current industrial progress.*

---

**The King's Armory**

A kingdom's craftsmen had grown so efficient that they produced more goods than could be sold at a profit. Workshops sat idle. The king's advisors told him the solution was simple: order more swords, more cannons, more warships. The craftsmen would be busy again, the merchants' ledgers would fill with orders, and prosperity would return.

It worked. The armory swallowed what the market would not. But each year the craftsmen grew more efficient, and each year the king needed to order more weapons to keep the workshops humming. The pile of unused swords grew enormous. The kingdom had never been more prosperous, or more heavily armed, or more puzzled about what to do with it all.

*If the wasteful expenditure slackens, the logical outcome should be a considerable perturbation of business and industry, followed by depression; if the waste comes to an abrupt stop, the logical consequence should be a crisis of some severity.*

---

**The Fisherman and the Clerk**

When trade was brisk, the fisherman worked longer hours and ate well, but the price of bread rose with everything else. The clerk, who lived on a fixed salary, watched his savings buy less each month.

When trade went slack, the fisherman's hours were cut and he suffered. But the clerk found that his same salary now stretched further -- bread was cheap, rent was low, his savings grew. He was quietly comfortable, though he took care not to say so.

*To those classes who live on a fixed income, dull times are a thinly disguised blessing. They gain in their ease of livelihood by all the difference between the price scale of brisk and of dull times. To these classes an era of prosperity brings substantially nothing but detriment.*

---

**The Benchmark That Moved**

All the merchants in a port town measured their wealth by the same stone weight on the harbor wall. They trusted it absolutely. They bought and sold, lent and borrowed, all by reference to the stone.

But the stone was sinking. Each year the harbor wall settled a little further into the mud, and the benchmark dropped with it. The merchants' measurements drifted, their accounts grew confused, their debts mismatched their assets. Yet not one of them would admit the stone had moved. It was the one fixed point in their world, and to question it was unthinkable.

One young trader saw the truth and profited enormously from it. The others thought him a scoundrel or a genius, they could not decide which.

*The conventional base line of business traffic is the money value, and a recognition of the patent fact that this base line wavers incontinently apparently exceeds the business man's practical powers of comprehension. Money value is his habitual bench-mark, and he holds to the conviction that this bench-mark is stable, in spite of the facts.*

---

**The Last Combination**

A crow, a fox, and a badger each ran a mill. They competed until none could profit. So they combined. For a time, earnings were fair.

But the crow's nephew built a new mill with cheaper stones, and the competition resumed. So they bought him out and combined again, larger this time. Then the fox's cousin started a mill. They bought her out too. Each round, the combination had to grow wider to hold.

At last every mill in the forest was under one roof, and still new capital came looking for a place to grind. The combination had to be total or it was nothing.

*The exigencies which enforce the resort to coalition uninterruptedly gain in scope and urgency; the trust must take the same course of growth to meet these exigencies, until the trust must comprehend in one close business coalition virtually the whole field of industry.*
