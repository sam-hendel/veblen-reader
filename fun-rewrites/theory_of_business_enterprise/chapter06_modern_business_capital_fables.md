# Chapter VI: Modern Business Capital — Fables

---

**The Mill and the Ledger**

A village had a fine stone mill that ground flour day and night. One day a merchant from the city arrived and asked to buy it.

"What is its price?" he said.

The miller answered, "It cost forty gold pieces to build, and every stone was laid by hand."

"I do not care what it cost," said the merchant. "I care what it earns."

He looked at the sacks of flour going out and the coins coming in, and he offered sixty gold pieces -- not for the stones, but for the stream of silver they produced.

The miller protested: "But the building is only worth forty!"

"The building is not the capital," said the merchant. "The earning is."

*As a business proposition, "capital" means a fund of money values, and this fund bears but a remote and fluctuating relation to the industrial equipment.*

**The Monastery's Seal**

Two monasteries stood on opposite hills. Both made the same fine cheese from the same breed of goat. But the elder monastery had something the younger did not: a wax seal, known across three kingdoms, stamped on every wheel.

A traveling abbot visited both. "Your cheese is identical," he said. "Yet the elder house is valued at ten times the younger. Why?"

The elder prior smiled. "The seal, the name, the old trade routes -- these are our true foundation. The goats are merely goats."

The abbot frowned. "But your seal feeds no one. It serves only your treasury."

"Precisely," said the prior.

*Good-will taken in its wider meaning comprises such things as established customary business relations, reputation, franchises, trade-marks, and patent rights. All these items give a differential advantage to their owners, but they are of no aggregate advantage to the community.*

**The Fox Who Held the Deed**

A crow, a badger, and a fox pooled their savings to buy a fishing boat. The crow and the badger each put in real coin -- enough to buy the hull, the nets, and the salt. In return they received certificates promising a steady share of the catch.

The fox put in nothing but his reputation and his knowledge of the river. For this he received a different kind of certificate -- one that gave him the right to steer the boat wherever he wished.

The crow and the badger owned the boat. But the fox decided where it went, when it sailed, and what was done with the fish.

"We paid for everything," said the crow. "Why does the fox command?"

"Because," said the badger slowly, "our certificates promise us a fixed portion. His certificate promises him the wheel."

*The discretionary disposal of the entire capital vests in securities representing the intangible assets. The owners of the industrial material have no voice in its management.*

---

**The Captain and the Ship**

A sea captain was given command of a great merchant vessel. He did not own it -- the owners were many, scattered across distant ports, known to him only as names on a register.

The captain studied the cargo markets. He saw that the ship was worth more in rumor than in lumber. So instead of sailing profitable routes, he let the ship drift into poor waters, and when the owners grew frightened and sold their shares cheaply, the captain bought them up. Then he sailed well again, and the ship's name recovered its luster, and the captain sold his shares at twice what he had paid.

The ship was no better and no worse than before. Only its reputation had changed, and the captain had arranged both the decline and the recovery.

*The interest of the managers is removed by one degree from that of the concerns under their management, and by two degrees from the interests of the community at large.*

**The Baker, the Bread, and the Betting Ring**

A baker made good bread and sold it at the market. His bakery earned steadily.

Then a group of merchants began trading not in bread, but in shares of the bakery itself. They bought and sold these shares among themselves, and the price rose and fell not with the quality of the bread, but with rumors -- a dry harvest, a new oven, the baker's health.

The baker noticed that the merchants cared nothing for his loaves. They cared only for what other merchants believed about his loaves tomorrow.

"Your bread is beside the point," one merchant told him. "What matters is the presumptive future earning-capacity."

The baker went back to his ovens, bewildered.

*The earning-capacity which affords ground for the valuation of marketable capital is not its past or actual earning-capacity, but its presumptive future earning-capacity; so that the fluctuations in the capital market turn about imagined future events.*

**The Weathervane Market**

In a certain kingdom, the value of every farm, forge, and fishing fleet was written on a great board in the town square. Each morning the numbers changed.

A blacksmith asked the recorder, "My forge is the same as yesterday. My iron is the same. My fire burns as hot. Why is my number lower today?"

"Because a rumor spread that the king may go to war," said the recorder. "And yesterday a frost was seen in the north."

"But I have the same forge!"

"The number is not about your forge," said the recorder. "It is about how people feel this morning."

*Under modern conditions the magnitude of business capital and its mutations from day to day are in great measure a question of folk psychology rather than of material fact.*

**The Conjurer's Good Name**

A renowned conjurer traveled from town to town, organizing fairs. At each fair, his name drew crowds, and the fair prospered. The townspeople, grateful, gave him a share of the profits.

A scholar observed that the conjurer's name had been counted as wealth in every town he had visited -- simultaneously. "How can one man's reputation be the foundation of ten different fairs at once?" the scholar asked.

"Easily," said the conjurer. "Good-will on this level has a certain character of inexhaustibility. Its use and capitalization in one place need not diminish the extent to which it may be used and capitalized in any other."

And indeed the fact of his name being attached to one fair seemed rather to augment than to diminish the price at which the next town would bid for it.

*Like other good-will, though perhaps in a higher degree of sublimation, it is of a spiritual nature, such that the whole of it may undividedly be present in every part of the various structures which it has created.*

**The Two Degrees of the Fish Market**

In an old fishing village, each fisherman owned his own boat and sold his own catch. If he caught poorly, he suffered. If he caught well, the village ate. His interest and the village's interest were close cousins, if not twins.

Then a new arrangement came. A trading house bought the boats and hired captains to sail them. Now the captains' interest was in the trading house's profit, not the village's supper. The village's welfare was one step removed.

Then a newer arrangement came still. Merchants in a distant city began trading shares in the trading house itself, and the men who controlled these shares cared nothing for the captains, the boats, or the fish. They cared only for the price of shares tomorrow versus today.

The village noticed that its harbor was now governed by men who had never seen the sea.

*Under the regime of the more adequately developed credit economy, with vendible corporate capital, the interest of the men who hold the discretion in industrial affairs is removed by two degrees from the interests of the community at large.*

**The Whale and the Minnows**

A whale and a school of minnows both swam in the same bay. Each minnow owned a tiny coral -- its home and livelihood. The whale owned a reef.

But the whale's reef gave him more than shelter. It gave him command of the current. He could steer the tides of the bay, and when he did, the minnows' corals rose or fell in value -- though nothing about the corals themselves had changed.

The risks of the whale's maneuvering fell not on the whale, but on the minnows. And the whale, who could foresee the currents he himself created, always swam to safety before the surge arrived.

A wise old turtle observed: "The manipulations carry risk, not so much to the manipulators, but to the concerns whose properties are the subject of manipulation. So secure and lucrative is this class of business that it is chiefly out of such traffic that the great modern fortunes are accumulated. Nothing so effective for the accumulation of private wealth is known to the history of human culture."
