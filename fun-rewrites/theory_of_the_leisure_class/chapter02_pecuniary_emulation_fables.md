# Chapter 2: Pecuniary Emulation — Fables

---

**The Wolf and His Trophies**

A wolf dragged a deer carcass back to the pack and left it in plain view at the mouth of his den. He did not eat it. When another wolf asked why, he said, "I have already eaten." The carcass rotted slowly in the sun. Other wolves began dragging their own kills to their dens and leaving them out. Soon the hillside was lined with rotting meat that no one touched.

A passing crow observed: "They do not want the flesh. They want the heap."

*The motive that lies at the root of ownership is emulation; and the same motive of emulation continues active in the further development of the institution to which it has given rise.*

---

**The Barn Full of Grain**

A mouse stored grain in a hollow log until the log was full. He felt content for a single evening. Then he noticed that his neighbour had filled two logs. So he set about filling a third, and then a fourth. His neighbour, seeing this, began to dig out a burrow beneath a granary. Neither mouse could eat a fraction of what he hoarded, but neither could stop.

A fieldmouse who owned nothing watched them from a stone wall and said: "They will never finish."

*The desire for wealth can scarcely be satiated in any individual instance. Since the struggle is substantially a race for reputability on the basis of an invidious comparison, no approach to a definitive attainment is possible.*

---

**The Ox and the Stallion**

An ox pulled a plow from dawn until dark and fed his family from the furrow. A stallion in the next field did no work at all but wore a fine bridle and stood in good pasture. The other animals esteemed the stallion and ignored the ox.

The ox's calf asked, "Father, why do they admire him and not you?"

The ox had no answer.

*With the growth of settled industry, the possession of wealth gains in relative importance and effectiveness as a customary basis of repute and esteem. It becomes the conventional basis of esteem.*

---

**The Two Roosters**

Two roosters lived in the same yard. The first could crow louder, fight harder, and find more seed. The second had inherited a large dunghill from his sire. The hens gathered around the dunghill. The first rooster fought and crowed and found seed, but the hens stayed where they were.

He asked an old hen why. She said: "The dunghill is there for all to see. Your crowing is over by morning."

*The possession of wealth, which was at the outset valued simply as an evidence of efficiency, becomes, in popular apprehension, itself a meritorious act. Wealth is now itself intrinsically honourable and confers honour on its possessor.*

---

**The Monk Who Could Not Pray**

A monk in a poor monastery owned only his robe and his beads. The other monks had each acquired a second robe, then a cushion, then a carved stool. He told himself he did not need these things. But he found he could not settle into prayer. His mind returned always to the carved stools.

He went to the abbot and confessed his distraction. The abbot said: "It is not the stool you want. It is that you cannot sit at ease among men who have what you lack."

*In any community where goods are held in severalty it is necessary, in order to his own peace of mind, that an individual should possess as large a portion of goods as others with whom he is accustomed to class himself.*

---

**The Hedgehog on the Hill**

A hedgehog climbed a hill and looked down at the other hedgehogs below. He felt satisfaction. Then he noticed a higher hill with a hedgehog already on it. He climbed that hill. From the top he saw a ridge, and on the ridge another hedgehog. He set off again. He climbed until his legs ached, and each time he reached a new height, he saw another rise beyond it.

He never stopped climbing. He never once looked at the view.

*The tendency in any case is constantly to make the present pecuniary standard the point of departure for a fresh increase of wealth; and this in turn gives rise to a new standard of sufficiency and a new pecuniary classification of one's self as compared with one's neighbours.*

---

**The Goose Who Fell Behind**

A goose in a flock could not keep up with the others in gathering reeds for her nest. Her nest was thin and small. The other geese began to walk past her without greeting. After a season of this she found she could not hold her head up even when she was alone.

A toad who lived under her nest asked, "Why do you droop when no one is watching?"

She said, "Their eyes are always watching, even when they are not here."

*Those members of the community who fall short of this normal degree of property suffer in the esteem of their fellow-men; and consequently they suffer also in their own esteem, since the usual basis of self-respect is the respect accorded by one's neighbours.*

---

**The Beaver and the Dam**

A beaver built a dam across a stream. It served its purpose: the pond was deep, the lodge was safe. But the beaver kept building. He added height, then length, then decorative branches that did nothing. Another beaver asked him why.

"I am not building a dam," he said. "I am building something that shows I can build."

The other beaver considered this and went to add a useless wing to his own dam.

*Under the regime of individual ownership the most available means of visibly achieving a purpose is that afforded by the acquisition and accumulation of goods. The propensity for achievement — the instinct of workmanship — tends more and more to shape itself into a straining to excel others in pecuniary achievement.*
