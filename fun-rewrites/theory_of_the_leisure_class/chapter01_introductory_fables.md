# Chapter 1: Introductory — Fables

---

**The Lion and the Ox**

A Lion and an Ox lived side by side on the same hillside. The Ox ploughed from dawn to dusk, turning the earth and dragging stones for the wall. The Lion spent his mornings sharpening his claws on a rock and his afternoons sleeping in the sun. When the rains came and the field yielded grain, the Lion took what he pleased from the heap. "I do not plough," he said. "Ploughing is for oxen." The Ox, who had watched the Lion do nothing all season but yawn and stretch, said nothing. He noticed, however, that the other animals on the hill had begun to bow to the Lion and not to him.

*The upper classes are by custom exempt or excluded from industrial occupations, and this exemption is the economic expression of their superior rank.*

---

**The Hunter and the Basket-Weaver**

A Hunter returned to camp carrying nothing. His wife had spent the day weaving baskets and drying fish. "Where is the deer?" she asked. "It escaped," said the Hunter, "but the chase was magnificent. I ran it across three ridges." He described each ridge in detail. The other men of the camp listened with admiration. The wife's baskets, full of dried fish that would feed the camp for a week, sat in a pile that no one remarked upon.

*His work may conduce to the maintenance of the group, but it is felt that it does so through an excellence and an efficacy of a kind that cannot without derogation be compared with the uneventful diligence of the women.*

---

**The Two Villages**

There were two villages of mice. The first village was small and quiet. The mice there shared their grain and seldom fought. They had no chief and no warriors. When a cat wandered near, they hid together and waited. Travellers who passed through found them pleasant but remarked that they would not last. The second village had a mighty mouse who had killed a shrew. He wore the shrew's skull on a string and ate first at every meal. The other mice brought him seeds. He did no gathering himself. Travellers who passed through called this village prosperous and well-governed.

*The most notable trait common to members of such communities is a certain amiable inefficiency when confronted with force or fraud.*

---

**The Falcon and the Hen**

A Falcon perched on a barn roof, watching a Hen scratch in the dirt below. "What are you doing?" asked the Falcon. "Finding grubs for my chicks," said the Hen. The Falcon laughed. "I seize my food from the air. I strike and it is mine." The Hen went on scratching. That evening the farmer's children fed the Falcon scraps of meat on a gloved hand and spoke of how noble he was. The Hen they did not mention, though her eggs were on their table every morning.

*Any effort that does not involve an assertion of prowess comes to be unworthy of the man.*

---

**The Bear Who Would Not Carry His Own Kill**

A Bear killed a salmon in the river. It was a fine salmon, fat and silver. But the Bear would not carry it back to his den. He sat on the bank and waited until a smaller bear came along, and made her drag it up the hill. "Why not carry it yourself?" asked a Crow from a branch. "You are strong enough." The Bear looked at the Crow as though the question were beneath him. He had caught the fish. That was the thing. Carrying it was another matter entirely.

*So tenaciously and with such nicety is this theoretical distinction between exploit and drudgery adhered to that in many hunting tribes the man must not bring home the game which he has killed, but must send his woman to perform that baser office.*

---

**The Stag's Antlers**

A young Stag grew his first set of antlers and immediately picked a fight with a fence post. He won. He then picked a fight with a sapling. He won again. By autumn he had scraped his antlers against every tree in the grove and stood in the clearing with his head high, waiting to be admired. An old Doe, watching from the thicket, observed that he had not once gone looking for water or good grass. But the other stags gathered around him, and when they spoke of worth, they spoke of the scars on his antlers.

*Visible success becomes an end sought for its own utility as a basis of esteem. Esteem is gained and dispraise is avoided by putting one's efficiency in evidence.*

---

**The Trophies of the Jackdaw**

A Jackdaw found a bright ring in the road and carried it to his nest. The other birds noticed. He found a brass button and brought that home too. Then a bit of red thread, then a chip of glass. Soon his nest glittered. The other jackdaws, who had built warm, sturdy nests lined with moss, began pulling moss out and replacing it with whatever shiny things they could find. By winter, every nest in the colony was cold and full of junk, but each bird could point to his pile and say, "Look what I have taken."

*Booty, trophies of the chase or of the raid, come to be prized as evidence of pre-eminent force. Aggression becomes the accredited form of action, and booty serves as prima facie evidence of successful aggression.*

---

**The Wolves Who Learned to Fight**

For a long time, a pack of wolves lived by hunting rabbits in a wide meadow. There were enough rabbits, and the wolves were content. Then the meadow shrank, and another pack moved close. The first skirmishes were confused — no wolf quite knew whether he was hunting or fighting. But as the encounters continued, the wolves began to see everything as a fight: the rabbit hunt, the river crossing, the argument over a bone. The wolves who were best at biting rose in the pack. Those who were best at finding rabbits sank. No one decided this. It simply happened, once the fight became the foreground of every day's thinking.

*The predatory phase of culture is attained only when the predatory attitude has become the habitual and accredited spiritual attitude for the members of the group; when the fight has become the dominant note in the current theory of life.*

---

**The Monk and the Blacksmith**

A Monk and a Blacksmith lived in the same town. The Blacksmith made ploughs, nails, hinges, and horseshoes. The Monk rang bells, chanted prayers, and blessed fields. At harvest time, the townspeople brought gifts to the Monk. The Blacksmith, whose ploughs had turned every furrow in every field, received his ordinary fee. "But I made the tools," said the Blacksmith. "Yes," said a townsman, "and we are grateful. But the Monk's work is of a different kind." No one could say precisely what the difference was, only that it was felt, and deeply.

*A distinction is still habitually made between industrial and non-industrial occupations; and this modern distinction is a transmuted form of the barbarian distinction between exploit and drudgery.*

---

**The Storm and the Potato**

A great storm came down the valley, tearing up trees and flooding the river. The animals cowered. When it passed, they spoke of it with awe, as though it had meant something. The next day, a potato quietly put out a root in the field. No one spoke of the potato. It was, after all, just a potato. But the storm — the storm was like a living thing, a force with a will. One of the older animals, a tortoise, observed that the potato would feed someone and the storm had only broken things, but no one was much interested in this opinion.

*To the mind of the barbarian, the elaboration and utilisation of what is afforded by inert nature is activity on quite a different plane from his dealings with "animate" things and forces.*

---

**The King's Epithet**

A King wished to be remembered and asked his court poet for a title. The poet considered: the King had built three bridges, reformed the tax rolls, and drained a swamp. "Shall I call you the Bridge-Builder?" asked the poet. "Certainly not," said the King. The poet tried again: "The Wise Administrator?" The King scowled. Finally the poet called him "The Crusher of Enemies, the Terrible, the Destroyer of Cities," though the King had fought only one small skirmish and lost his horse in it. The King was pleased. The bridges stood for a hundred years, but no one put them in the song.

*Honorific epithets, in vogue among barbarian tribes as well as among peoples of a more advanced culture, commonly bear the stamp of this unsophisticated sense of honour.*
