# Chapter 3: Conspicuous Leisure — Fables

---

**The Rich Mole Who Dug at Night**

A mole had accumulated the largest store of roots and grubs in the whole meadow. But he kept his tunnels hidden and his hoards underground, and went on digging quietly each day. The other animals passed him without a glance. Meanwhile, a jay who owned far less sat on the highest branch all morning, preening and calling. Every creature in the meadow knew the jay and deferred to him.

At last the mole complained: "I have twice what that bird has. Why does no one respect me?"

An old toad replied: "Because no one has ever seen it."

*In order to gain and to hold the esteem of men it is not sufficient merely to possess wealth or power. The wealth or power must be put in evidence, for esteem is awarded only on evidence.*

---

**The Ox Who Would Not Pull**

An ox of noble lineage was brought to a farm where all the animals worked. The farmer yoked him to a plough. The ox stood still. The farmer beat him. The ox stood still. The other animals said: "He will starve if he does not pull." But the ox had been raised in a pasture where no beast of good blood ever wore a yoke, and his father and grandfather before him had never pulled. He stood in his traces, growing thin, and at last fell dead in the furrow rather than drag the plough one yard.

The farmer shook his head. But the other noble oxen in the neighbouring fields, hearing the story, said he had done the only honourable thing.

*Prescription ends by making labour not only disreputable in the eyes of the community, but morally impossible to the noble, freeborn man, and incompatible with a worthy life.*

---

**The Impoverished Crane**

A crane of ancient family lost her fishing grounds to a flood. She stood on the bank, gaunt and hungry, while common herons waded in the shallows catching minnows. A sparrow urged her: "Go fish with them. You will eat tonight."

"I cannot," said the crane. "My mother never fished in common water, nor her mother before her."

She stood on the bank through the summer and into the autumn, growing thinner and more dignified with each passing week, until she was nothing but feathers and pride. The herons, well-fed, watched her with a mixture of pity and admiration.

*Wherever the canon of conspicuous leisure has a chance undisturbed to work out its tendency, there will therefore emerge a secondary, and in a sense spurious, leisure class — abjectly poor and living a precarious life of want and discomfort, but morally unable to stoop to gainful pursuits.*

---

**The Monk Who Knew Dead Languages**

Two monks arrived at a monastery on the same day. The first could repair roofs, mend fences, and brew good beer. The second could do none of these things, but he read ancient Greek and could recite the genealogies of kings long dead. He also played the lute, though badly.

Within a year the second monk sat at the abbot's table, while the first monk worked in the yard. A visiting friar asked why.

The abbot explained: "Any labourer can fix a roof. But only a man who has never needed to fix a roof has had the years to learn what Brother Marcus knows. His useless knowledge is the proof of his quality."

*Such immaterial evidences of past leisure are quasi-scholarly or quasi-artistic accomplishments and a knowledge of processes and incidents which do not conduce directly to the furtherance of human life.*

---

**The Goat Who Forgot to Bow**

In a barnyard governed by an old ram, every animal bowed when the ram passed and stepped aside when he approached the trough. A young goat, newly arrived from a wild hill, ate without waiting and walked without bowing. He meant no offence. He simply did not know the custom.

The other animals were horrified — not because the goat had taken food, but because he had taken it without ceremony. The hens would not roost near him. The dogs would not walk with him. Even the pigs turned away. When the goat asked what he had done wrong, a sheep answered: "We could forgive a thief before we could forgive a boor."

*A breach of faith may be condoned, but a breach of decorum can not.*

---

**The Badger's Busy Household**

A badger grew prosperous and took a mate. He then announced that his wife would no longer dig for roots. Instead, she would arrange the stones at the entrance of their den, sort the leaves of their bedding by size, and keep the tunnels swept to a standard no visitor could fail to notice. The wife worked from dawn to dusk at these arrangements, harder than she had ever worked at digging.

A passing fox observed: "Your wife does more labour than any beast in the wood."

"That may be," said the badger, "but none of it is productive."

And this was precisely the point.

*The vicarious leisure performed by housewives and menials, under the head of household cares, may frequently develop into drudgery, especially where the competition for reputability is close and strenuous.*

---

**The King and His Clumsy Cupbearer**

A king hired a new cupbearer, a strong young man from a farm. The youth poured wine without spilling a drop and served every dish promptly. But he held the pitcher like a milk pail. He set down plates as though feeding hogs. He stood at attention the way a man stands behind a plough — stiff, square, and ready to work.

The courtiers winced. The king dismissed him within a week and hired a thin, slow man who had served in noble houses since boyhood. This man did less, and did it with exquisite uselessness.

"The farm boy was more efficient," said a visiting duke.

"That was the problem," said the king. "His service suggested he had spent his life doing something useful."

*It is a serious grievance if a gentleman's butler or footman performs his duties in such unformed style as to suggest that his habitual occupation may be ploughing or sheepherding. If the performance of the servant argues lack of means on the part of his master, it defeats its chief substantial end; for the chief use of servants is the evidence they afford of the master's ability to pay.*

---

**The Stag with Many Attendants**

A great stag kept a retinue of smaller deer who did nothing but stand near him. One carried a leaf but never set it down. Another walked behind him but opened no path. A third stood at the entrance to his clearing, turning no one away, for no one came.

A wolf passing through asked: "What do all these deer do?"

"They attend me," said the stag.

"But what do they do?" the wolf pressed.

The stag looked puzzled by the question. The whole point was that they did nothing. A deer who produced something would have been no ornament at all. Their magnificent idleness was the stag's wealth made visible.

*There supervenes a division of labour among the servants whose life is spent in maintaining the honour of the gentleman of leisure. So that, while one group produces goods for him, another group consumes for him in conspicuous leisure; thereby putting in evidence his ability to sustain large pecuniary damage without impairing his superior opulence.*
