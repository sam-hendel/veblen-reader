# Chapter 5: The Pecuniary Standard of Living -- Fables

---

**The Fox Who Could Not Go Back to His Den**

A fox lived in a simple burrow for years and was content. Then he discovered an abandoned badger's sett -- larger, with three chambers and a dry sleeping room. He moved in at once. A season later, a flood collapsed the sett, and he was forced back to his old burrow. But now the burrow felt intolerable. The ceiling pressed on his ears. The single chamber seemed to smell. He could not sleep. Nothing about the burrow had changed; it was exactly as he had left it. But the fox had changed. He dug frantically at the walls, trying to widen what had once been enough. He would rather sleep cold in a half-finished excavation than warm in the home that had served him perfectly well the year before.

*It is much more difficult to recede from a scale of expenditure once adopted than it is to extend the accustomed scale in response to an accession of wealth.*

---

**The Geese on the River**

A flock of geese swam in a river, loosely sorted by size. Each goose watched the goose just ahead of her and tried to match its pace and bearing. The smallest goose at the rear watched the goose in front of her, who watched the one in front of her, and so on up the line, until at the front swam the great gander who set the pace for all. No goose compared herself to the gander directly -- he was too far ahead. And no goose looked back at those behind her -- what would be the point? But because each watched the one just above, the gander's every turn eventually rippled through the whole flock, down to the smallest bird at the rear, who believed she was only keeping up with her neighbor.

*Each class envies and emulates the class next above it in the social scale, while it rarely compares itself with those below or with those who are considerably in advance. All standards of consumption are traced back by insensible gradations to the usages and habits of thought of the highest social and pecuniary class.*

**The Miser and the Village**

A merchant's trade doubled in a single season. His neighbors watched for the new coat, the better horse, the feast. None came. The merchant continued to eat barley soup and wear his old boots. At first the village was puzzled. Then it grew suspicious. Then it grew contemptuous. "He must be hiding something," they said. "He must be afraid." No one accused him of theft or cruelty -- only of failing to do what any decent man would do with new money. His thrift, which would have been called wisdom in a poor man, was called miserliness in one who could afford otherwise. He had not injured anyone. He had merely failed to spend, and this the village could not forgive.

*A failure to increase one's visible consumption when the means for an increase are at hand is felt in popular apprehension to call for explanation, and unworthy motives of miserliness are imputed to those who fall short in this respect.*

**The Donkey on the Treadmill**

A donkey turned a millstone that ground grain for the village. A clever tinker improved the mechanism so that the same amount of grain could be ground with half the effort. The donkey's owner was pleased -- but not because the donkey might rest. He hitched the donkey to a second millstone. Now the donkey ground twice as much grain as before, with the same effort as the old arrangement. When a second tinker improved the mechanism again, a third millstone was added. The donkey never rested more than he had before the first improvement. Each gain in efficiency was swallowed immediately by an expansion of what was expected. The donkey, for his part, could not remember a time when the work had been lighter.

*The strain is not lightened as industrial efficiency increases and makes a lighter strain possible, but the increment of output is turned to use to meet this want, which is indefinitely expansible. "Hitherto it is questionable if all the mechanical inventions yet made have lightened the day's toil of any human being."*

**The Crabs in the Pot**

A crab tried to climb out of a fisherman's pot. The other crabs pulled him back. They did not pull him back from malice, exactly, but from a deep unease at seeing one of their number depart from the common condition. Among the crabs, the standard of life was the pot. A crab who refused to live as the others lived was not admired for independence but suspected of contempt for his fellows. The pot was uncomfortable, but it was the known way. To accept and practice the standard of living which is in vogue is both agreeable and expedient, and to depart from it is to risk something worse than discomfort.

*To accept and practice the standard of living which is in vogue is both agreeable and expedient, commonly to the point of being indispensable to personal comfort and to success in life.*

**The Monk's Parlor and the Monk's Cell**

A monk kept two rooms. His parlor, where visitors were received, was furnished with carved chairs, a polished table, and a good candle. His cell, where he actually slept and ate, contained a straw pallet and a tin cup. A visitor who saw only the parlor would think the monk lived well. A visitor who saw only the cell would think him destitute. The monk was neither. He was a man who had learned, as everyone learns, which parts of life are seen and which are not, and who spent accordingly. The parlor was for the world. The cell was for himself. In time, even the monk forgot which room was his real life and which was the performance.

*Through this discrimination in favor of visible consumption it has come about that the domestic life of most classes is relatively shabby, as compared with the eclat of that overt portion of their life that is carried on before the eyes of observers.*

**The Scholar's Thin Coat**

A scholar was admitted to the company of lords, for his learning was rare and his reputation high. He sat at their tables and walked in their gardens. But his stipend was a scholar's stipend, not a lord's income. The lords spent freely on horses, banquets, and fine cloth, and the scholar, seated among them, felt the pressure to do the same. He could not. Yet he could not appear poor, either, for that would disgrace his station. So he spent what he had on the visible things -- a decent coat, a proper hat, gifts when gifts were expected -- and economized savagely on everything else. He ate little. He kept no fire in winter. He had no children, for he could not maintain them in the style his position required. Of all the men at the lord's table, none spent a larger share of his substance on keeping up appearances, and none had less left over for the substance of life.

*In any modern community where there is no priestly monopoly of these occupations, the people of scholarly pursuits are unavoidably thrown into contact with classes that are pecuniarily their superiors. There is no class of the community that spends a larger proportion of its substance in conspicuous waste than these.*

**The Old Dog's Bone**

An old dog had buried bones in seven places around the yard. When lean times came, he dug them up one by one, starting with the most recent. The last bone he would surrender -- the one buried deepest and longest ago -- was the first bone he had ever hidden, back when he was a pup with nothing. It was small and nearly bare. But it was the oldest habit he had, and he would starve before he gave it up. A younger dog, watching, asked why he did not eat the oldest bone first, since it was the least nourishing. The old dog could not explain it. He only knew that the most ancient habits, the ones that touch existence itself, are the most persistent and imperative, and that they are given up last.

*The most ancient and ingrained of the habits which govern the individual's life -- those habits that touch his existence as an organism -- are the most persistent and imperative.*

---

**The Raven Who Found Silver**

A raven discovered a cache of silver coins in a fallen tree. He had no use for silver -- he could not eat it, nest in it, or fly with it. But he had long watched the magpies, who collected bright things and were admired for their hoards. The propensity was already in him, waiting. Within a day he was arranging the coins by size. Within a week he was guarding them from other birds. Within a month he had forgotten that he had ever lived without them. He did not decide to become a hoarder. The capacity was ancient and deep, and the silver merely gave it a direction. Any increase in means, where the habit of display is already in the air, will flow into the channels that the old propensities have already carved.

*Whenever an accession of pecuniary strength puts the individual in a position to unfold his life process in larger scope, the ancient propensities of the race will assert themselves in determining the direction which the new unfolding of life is to take.*
