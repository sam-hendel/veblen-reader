# Chapter 4: Conspicuous Consumption -- Fables

---

**The Lion's Feast and the Jackal's Fast**

A lion killed a great antelope and dragged it to the shade of a baobab tree. He ate the choicest cuts slowly, leaving much to rot. A jackal, watching from a distance, crept forward to gnaw at what remained. The lion snarled and drove him off. "But you cannot finish it," said the jackal. "That is beside the point," said the lion. The vultures, too, were permitted to circle but not to land. The meat spoiled in the sun. The lion slept beside it, satisfied. The other animals understood: the antelope was not food. It was a declaration.

*Unproductive consumption of goods is honourable, primarily as a mark of prowess and a perquisite of human dignity; secondarily it becomes substantially honourable in itself, especially the consumption of the more desirable things.*

---

**The Monk Who Learned to Taste**

A monk was elevated to abbot. He had lived on bread and water, and this had suited him. But now he was expected to preside over a table of wines and roasted meats. At first he ate without distinction, swallowing whatever was placed before him. The other abbots whispered. He was sent to study under a retired bishop, who taught him to tell a Burgundy from a Bordeaux, to judge the age of cheese, and to find fault with bread that was merely good. It took him three years. When he returned, he could no longer eat plain bread without wincing. He had acquired, at great effort, the inability to enjoy what he had once been perfectly happy with.

*He becomes a connoisseur in creditable viands of various degrees of merit, in manly beverages and trinkets, in seemly apparel and architecture. This cultivation of aesthetic faculty requires time and application, and the demands made upon the gentleman in this direction therefore tend to change his life of leisure into a more or less arduous application to the business of learning how to live a life of ostensible leisure in a becoming way.*

**The Feast of the Two Chieftains**

Two chieftains lived on opposite banks of a river. The first chieftain slaughtered twenty goats and invited the second to eat. The second chieftain came, and ate until he could eat no more, and still there were heaps of meat untouched. He went home in a foul temper. The next month he slaughtered thirty goats and invited the first chieftain across. And so it went, back and forth, the herds growing thinner on both sides of the river. Neither chieftain was hungry. Neither particularly enjoyed goat. But each needed the other to sit at his table and witness what he could not consume alone.

*The competitor with whom the entertainer wishes to institute a comparison is, by this method, made to serve as a means to the end. He consumes vicariously for his host at the same time that he is witness to the consumption of that excess of good things which his host is unable to dispose of single-handed.*

**The Carpenter's Wife**

A carpenter worked from dawn to dusk, six days a week. His hands were rough and his clothes were plain. But his wife wore a fine woolen cloak and kept the house in a state of spotless and elaborate order, polishing brass that needed no polishing, arranging linen that no one would see. The carpenter could not afford to rest, for someone in the household had to show that the household could afford rest. He worked with the utmost assiduity so that she might, in due form, render for him that degree of visible leisure which was expected of a respectable family. She was not idle. She was busy at the particular kind of work that proves, on analysis, to serve little or no end beyond showing that she does not occupy herself with anything gainful.

*It is by no means an uncommon spectacle to find a man applying himself to work with the utmost assiduity, in order that his wife may in due form render for him that degree of vicarious leisure which the common sense of the time demands.*

**The Sparrows and the Peacock**

A peacock strutted through a farmyard. The sparrows watched. One sparrow found a small blue feather and tucked it behind her wing. Another sparrow, seeing this, found a bit of colored thread and did the same. Soon every sparrow in the yard wore some scrap of finery, each imitating the one slightly above her in the dust-bath hierarchy. The peacock did not notice. The sparrows could not fly as well with their decorations, and they spent time arranging them that might have been spent finding food. But not one sparrow removed her ornament. Even the most bedraggled sparrow in the lowest corner of the yard kept her thread.

*The members of each stratum accept as their ideal of decency the scheme of life in vogue in the next higher stratum, and bend their energies to live up to that ideal. No class of society, not even the most abjectly poor, forgoes all customary conspicuous consumption.*

**The Two Potters**

Two potters made equally good pots. One lived in a small village where everyone knew his house, his clay stores, and how many days he rested each week. The other lived in a great market city where no one knew him at all. The village potter kept a tidy shop and took long afternoons off, and his neighbors respected him, for they could see his ease. The city potter found that no one cared how much he rested -- strangers passing his stall could not see his leisure, only his wares. So he began to glaze his pots in elaborate patterns and fire them twice, though once was enough. His pots cost more and held no more water. But passersby could see them, and seeing them, formed their judgment in the moment of passing.

*The only practicable means of impressing one's pecuniary ability on these unsympathetic observers of one's everyday life is an unremitting demonstration of ability to pay. The signature of one's pecuniary strength should be written in characters which he who runs may read.*

**The Hen and the China Eggs**

A hen was given a nest of china eggs to sit upon. She sat with great devotion, turning them carefully, fluffing her feathers over them, and driving off any creature that came near. She sat for weeks. No chicks hatched. But her sitting was earnest and diligent and she could not be faulted for lack of effort. A rooster, observing this, remarked that her brooding proved nothing about the eggs but everything about the instinct. The instinct of workmanship is present in all creatures, and asserts itself even under very adverse circumstances. When there is no useful work permitted, it will settle on useless work and perform it with the same conviction.

*The fact that it may under stress of circumstances eventuate in inanities no more disproves the presence of the instinct than the reality of the brooding instinct is disproved by inducing a hen to sit on a nestful of china eggs.*

**The King's Tapestry**

A king ordered an enormous tapestry woven for his great hall. It took twelve weavers four years. It depicted a hunting scene of no particular accuracy. It kept out no cold that a plain curtain would not have stopped. A visiting minister admired it and asked whether it was useful. "Useful?" said the king. The minister corrected himself: "Beautiful, I mean." And indeed it was beautiful -- but its beauty was of the kind that requires someone to tell you what it cost before you can fully appreciate it. After a generation, no one in the castle could imagine the hall without it. What had begun as display had become necessity. To remove it would have felt like poverty.

*It frequently happens that an element of the standard of living which set out with being primarily wasteful, ends with becoming, in the apprehension of the consumer, a necessary of life; and it may in this way become as indispensable as any other item of the consumer's habitual expenditure.*
