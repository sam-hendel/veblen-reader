# Chapter 6: Pecuniary Canons of Taste — Fables

---

**The Two Spoons**

A silversmith and a tinker each made a spoon. The silversmith's was hand-wrought, heavy, and slightly uneven at the rim. The tinker's was pressed from common metal, lighter, and shaped so that soup never dripped from its lip. A merchant set them both on his table and invited his neighbors to sup.

Every guest reached for the silver spoon. "How beautiful," they said, turning it in the candlelight. The tinker's spoon sat untouched. "But try them both," said the merchant. Each guest did, and admitted the tin spoon carried broth to the mouth more cleanly. Still, not one of them chose it again.

Then the merchant played a trick. He had the silversmith cast an exact copy of his spoon in tin and plate it with silver. The guests admired it just as before — until one, scratching the surface, revealed the base metal beneath. At once the whole table declared the spoon ugly, though not a single line of its form had changed.

*"The superior gratification derived from the use and contemplation of costly and supposedly beautiful products is, commonly, in great measure a gratification of our sense of costliness masquerading under the name of beauty."*

**The Swindler's Feast**

A fox stole a great quantity of grain from the common storehouse. He was caught and brought before the elder animals. But before his trial, he used the stolen grain to throw an enormous feast for the whole village — roasted corn, fresh bread, and barley cakes laid out on long tables.

The rabbit, who had stolen only a single turnip the week before, had been driven from the village in disgrace. But the fox sat at the head of his lavish table, and the other animals found it difficult to speak harshly of him. "He is generous, at least," said the badger. "He has taste," said the crane. The charges were quietly reduced.

*"The thief or swindler who has gained great wealth by his delinquency has a better chance than the small thief of escaping the rigorous penalty of the law, and some good repute accrues to him from his increased wealth and from his spending the irregularly acquired possessions in a seemly manner."*

**The Uncomfortable Chapel**

The monks of a small order built a chapel on a hill. Brother Martin carved the benches with care and made them smooth and gently curved to support the back. The chapel was plain, sturdy, and warm.

Visitors from a wealthy monastery came and frowned. "This is no proper house of worship," they said. "The benches are too comfortable. The walls are too bare. It looks as though someone might actually rest here." They sent their own craftsmen, who replaced the benches with rigid, narrow pews, hung heavy and expensive tapestries that blocked the windows, and laid a stone floor that chilled every knee that touched it. The monks shivered through their prayers, but all agreed the chapel now felt holy.

*"It is felt by all men that a right and enlightened sense of the true, the beautiful, and the good demands that in all expenditure on the sanctuary anything that might serve the comfort of the worshipper should be conspicuously absent."*

**The Footman and the Priest**

A footman served a lord in a great house. He moved slowly, never hurrying, never showing effort. He carried dishes at a stately pace and poured wine with an air of perfect indifference. The lord was pleased, for the footman's idleness proved the lord could afford a servant who did almost nothing.

Across the square, a priest conducted the weekly rites. He recited the old formulas in a flat, unhurried drone, turning the pages without looking at them. His robes were stiff and heavy, and he moved as though walking through water. The congregation was pleased, for his perfunctory manner proved that the divinity he served was far above any need of actual, useful service.

A traveler, watching both, could not tell which performance was for which master.

*"It is needless to point out the close analogy at this point between the priestly office and the office of the footman."*

**The Gardener's Roses**

A gardener grew three kinds of roses. The first grew wild in the hedgerows and cost nothing. The second could be raised in any cottage plot with modest effort. The third required a glass house, imported soil, and years of careful breeding, though its blossoms were no more shapely or fragrant than the others.

The poor admired all three. The middling folk admired the second but called the first a weed. The wealthy admired only the third and called the other two vulgar. Yet if a traveler from far away, knowing nothing of their cost, were shown the three side by side, he could not have said which was the weed and which was the treasure.

*"A beautiful article which is not expensive is accounted not beautiful."*

**The Cow and the Deer**

A wealthy landowner had broad, green grounds around his estate. His steward suggested turning a few cows out to graze — they would keep the grass trim, and the sight of cattle on green pasture was, in truth, the loveliest thing a pastoral eye could wish for.

The landowner recoiled. "Cows? On my lawn? They would make the whole place look like a working farm." Instead, he purchased, at enormous cost, a small herd of ornamental deer. The deer were skittish, ate the wrong plants, and trampled the flower beds. But no one could accuse them of being useful.

*"The vulgar suggestion of thrift, which is nearly inseparable from the cow, is a standing objection to the decorative use of this animal."*

**The Dog and the Cat**

A cat and a dog lived in the same rich household. The cat caught mice in the cellar, kept herself clean, and regarded her master with the calm equality of one creature acknowledging another. The dog caught nothing, rolled in filth, and flung himself at his master's feet with frantic devotion every time the man entered a room.

The master loved the dog and merely tolerated the cat. Visitors praised the dog's loyalty and beauty. No one praised the cat. She was too useful, too independent, and too cheap to keep.

*"The cat is less reputable than the other two just named, because she is less wasteful; she may even serve a useful end."*

**The Bonnet Maker**

A bonnet maker crafted two hats of equal skill and material. One was this season's shape — tall, with a forward tilt. The other was last season's shape — wide, with a trailing ribbon. She set them both in her shop window.

Every woman who passed reached for the new one and declared it beautiful. The old one, they said, looked somehow wrong — clumsy, even ugly. The bonnet maker kept the rejected hat in storage. Twenty-five years later she put it in the window again. By then, neither shape was in fashion, and passersby, seeing both together, could not for the life of them say which was the lovelier.

*"When viewed in the perspective of a quarter of a century, it would, I apprehend, be a matter of the utmost difficulty to award the palm for intrinsic beauty to the one rather than to the other of these structures."*

**The Hand and the Machine**

Two weavers lived in the same town. One worked at a hand loom, slowly, and every cloth she made had small irregularities — a slightly uneven selvage, a faint wobble in the stripe. The other worked at a power loom and turned out cloth of perfect, uniform weave, faster and cheaper.

The wealthy buyers all chose the hand-woven cloth. "See this subtle variation," they said, running their fingers along the uneven edge. "This is the mark of true craft." The machine-woven cloth, though flawless, they dismissed as common.

But the margin of crudeness had to be just so. Too much irregularity and it looked like bungling — evidence of low cost. Too little and it looked like a machine had made it — also evidence of low cost. The hand weaver learned to fail in precisely the right degree.

*"The ground of the superiority of hand-wrought goods, therefore, is a certain margin of crudeness. This margin must never be so wide as to show bungling workmanship, since that would be evidence of low cost, nor so narrow as to suggest the ideal precision attained only by the machine, for that would be evidence of low cost."*

**The Candle and the Lamp**

For many years, the animals of a prosperous village lit their evening meals with candles, for candles were the cheapest light to be had. No one thought them particularly beautiful. Then a tinker brought oil lamps to the village, and later, gas, and later still, electric light — each brighter, steadier, and cheaper than the last. The candles were put away in drawers.

A generation passed. The children who grew up under electric light had never known candles as a necessity. One evening, a wealthy old badger set candles on his dinner table. "How soft," his guests murmured. "How gentle on the eyes. How refined." Soon every fashionable house in the village burned candles at supper, though they gave less light and cost more than the alternatives. The candles had not changed. Only their price had.

*"Candlelight is now softer, less distressing to well-bred eyes, than oil, gas, or electric light. The same could not have been said thirty years ago, when candles were, or recently had been, the cheapest available light for domestic use."*

**The Forest and the Saplings**

A farmer in a wooded country had a fine stand of oaks, elms, and beeches around his house — old trees, thick-trunked, alive with birds. His neighbor, visiting from the treeless plains, said he had never seen anything so handsome.

But the farmer was uneasy. The trees had cost him nothing. They had simply been there, growing, when he arrived. So he cut them all down and planted, at considerable expense, a row of young soft maples from a nursery catalog. The saplings were spindly and would not give proper shade for twenty years. But they had been purchased, planted with ceremony, and could be pointed out to visitors as an improvement.

*"It is felt that the inexpensiveness of leaving the forest trees standing would derogate from the dignity that should invest an article which is intended to serve a decorative and honorific end."*

**The Law of Survival**

An old crow sat on a fence post watching the village market. A young crow asked him, "Why do the animals buy such strange and useless things? Why do their customs persist when they serve no purpose?"

The old crow said: "Watch carefully. Every season, new fashions appear — new shapes of hats, new styles of fences, new ways of trimming a garden. Most arise for reasons that have nothing to do with waste. But only those fashions that happen to look expensive survive. The rest vanish. It is not that waste invents anything. It is that waste decides what lives and what dies."

*"The law of conspicuous waste does not account for the origin of variations, but only for the persistence of such forms as are fit to survive under its dominance. It acts to conserve the fit, not to originate the acceptable."*
