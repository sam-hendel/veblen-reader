# On the Nature of Capital: Fables

---

**The Hermit's Garden**

A hermit declared he would feed himself by his own hands alone, owing nothing to any village. He walked into the wilderness with no tools, no seeds, no rope, and no word for "fire." By the third day he was eating mud. A passing traveler, who had been taught by her mother to set snares, who had learned from her village how to weave cord, who carried a knife whose design was older than any living memory, shared her supper with him and said nothing.

*Neither an individual person nor a single household can maintain its life in isolation. Economically speaking, this is the characteristic trait of humanity that separates mankind from the other animals.*

---

**The Weaver's Loom**

A weaver built a loom of surpassing ingenuity. The whole town marveled at it. "I invented this," he said. But the wood was cut with an axe whose pattern came from his grandfather's country. The cord was twisted in a fashion taught to him by a woman from the south. The shuttle followed a design he had seen as a boy and half-forgotten. His own addition was a small notch in the frame that made the shuttle return more smoothly. It was a fine notch. It was not a loom.

*The invention or discovery so achieved always embodies so much of what is already given that the creative contribution of the inventor or discoverer is trivial by comparison.*

---

**The Broken Tribe**

A small tribe knew how to fire clay pots, how to smoke fish so it kept through winter, and how to make a certain blue dye from river stones. War scattered them. Those who survived joined other peoples in ones and twos. Within a generation no one alive could fire the clay properly. No one remembered the dye. The fish-smoking was half-recalled by an old woman, but she had no apprentice, and the people she lived among dried their fish in the sun and saw no reason to learn her way.

*Whenever a given cultural community is broken up or suffers a serious diminution of numbers, its technological heritage deteriorates and dwindles.*

---

**The Man Who Bought the River**

A river ran through a valley where a hundred families fished and irrigated their fields. A strong man built a fence across the river at a narrow gorge. He did not make the river. He did not teach anyone to fish. He did not invent irrigation. But the fence was at the only point where the water could pass, and now everyone who wished to use what they already knew how to use had to pay him for the privilege.

*It becomes feasible for the individual with the strong arm to engross the usufruct of the commonplace knowledge of ways and means by taking over such of the requisite material as may be relatively scarce and relatively indispensable.*

---

**The Flint Merchant's Heirs**

A family grew wealthy beyond measure trading flint, for flint was what every blade and axe required. They built storehouses. They married well. Their children's children inherited the storehouses and sat proudly among the flint. Then someone learned the trick of smelting copper. Within a few seasons the storehouses gathered dust, and the flint was carried off by children who liked the way it sparked when thrown against a wall.

*When a technological expedient is in this way eliminated, the material repository of it ceases to have value as capital.*

---

**The Miller Who Burned His Flour**

A miller owned the only mill in the county. When the harvest was large and flour plentiful, he found that prices fell so low his income suffered. So he milled only half the grain and left the rest to rot. The county went hungry, though the wheat was heaped in his yard. A neighbor asked him why. "I am not in the business of feeding people," said the miller. "I am in the business of owning a mill."

*It becomes pecuniarily expedient for the owner of the material equipment to curtail or retard the processes of industry. The motive is expediency in terms of income from investment, not expediency in terms of serviceability to the community.*

---

**The Arsenals**

Two kingdoms spent half their wealth building warships. The ships were marvels of craft — the best timber, the finest iron, the cleverest engineering their civilizations could produce. The ships faced each other across a strait for forty years, then rotted at anchor. A fisherman, watching the last hull break apart, remarked that the nails alone would have built every bridge his county lacked.

*There is a large volume of capital goods whose value lies in their turning the technological inheritance to the injury of mankind, without sensible offset or abatement.*

---

**The Whale and the Pilot Fish**

The old-fashioned merchants of a trading town — men who owned ships and warehouses and knew the price of every cargo — woke one morning to find that a single banker now held the notes on all their ships. He had not bought the ships. He had not learned the trade. He had simply bought the debts, combined them, and discovered that whoever controls enough debt controls the debtors. The merchants still ran their routes, still haggled at port, still fretted over weather and worms in the hull. But the profit drained upward with the quiet regularity of a tide. The banker's maneuvers were not clever. They were merely large.

*A dispassionate student of the current business traffic will be more impressed by the ease and simplicity of the maneuvers that lead to large pecuniary results than by any evidence of preeminent sagacity and initiative among the pecuniary magnates.*

---

**The Hen and the Nest**

A hen hatched her eggs. The chicks broke free and wandered into the yard, pecking at seeds. But the farmer, who had invested in the nest, in the straw, in the arrangement of the henhouse, could not bear to see the nest empty. He spent the rest of the season pushing the hen back onto the bare shells, hiring consultants to study the nest, and writing letters to other farmers about the importance of sitting. The chicks, meanwhile, were out in the yard, getting on with things.

*The boldest, wisest, most public-spirited, most illustrious gentlemen of our time are spending their manhood in an endeavor to make the hen continue sitting on the nest after the chickens are out of the shell.*
